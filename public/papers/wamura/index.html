Controlled Relaxation Protocols for the Wamura Problem

📄 Download PDF version

A restriction justified by a long-timeline risk cannot be revised through observation: the evidence that would revise it cannot arrive within any practical decision cycle, and waiting for the catastrophe that would generate the evidence is the failure mode the restriction was meant to prevent. We call this the Wamura problem. We formalize the structural asymmetry, prove that max-aggregation with neutral-prior initialization produces monotone overcaution absent external intervention, and introduce controlled relaxation protocols: bounded, monitored exercise windows that generate genuine Bayesian evidence about risk claims while provably bounding maximum damage. A scope-construction theorem bounds the damage from a test by a user-set parameter \epsilon even when cross-boundary cooperative capabilities could propagate the test’s effects. A convergence theorem shows that repeated controlled relaxation drives risk estimates almost surely to truth at exponential rate, with the rate given by the Kullback–Leibler divergence between the observation distributions. We extend the protocol to keystone-refinement tests, which handle the common case where a restriction is not obsolete but misconfigured: the underlying risk pathway still operates, but technological progress has made narrower keystones available that would block it without blocking adjacent benign pathways. The protocol closes the most important open problem named in  and resolves the degenerate-phase-boundary case of .

Introduction

Escalating capabilities within a society create escalating mechanisms for disaster the civilisation has never experienced, some existentially catastrophic if blindly accelerated toward. This capability-escalating regime is precisely the {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-maximising dynamic that Goal-Frontier Maximization (GFM) seeks to produce; the question this paper addresses is how to balance the existential risks of such escalation against the reciprocal danger of a society that becomes increasingly bound by restrictive overcaution, accumulating restrictions it cannot revise through observation, approaching the over-calibrated-restriction feedback loop of .

The classical balancing mechanism is catastrophe-as-teacher: a first occurrence writes historical markers—tsunami stones, disaster records, institutional memory, building-code revisions, pharmacovigilance regimes—that subsequent generations consult in lieu of the experiment they cannot run. This mechanism is unavailable for capabilities novel to any generation, which is precisely the regime the escalating dynamics generate. This paper constructs a formal alternative: a mechanism for generating the evidential content historical markers would have recorded, within a bounded damage budget chosen by the framework rather than the next generation.

The specific failure mode in the existing GFM framework is the max-aggregation pathology of , named there as the most important open problem of the sequence: the conservative max operator, combined with the verification asymmetry inherent in risk prevention, produces overcaution that is structurally irresolvable within the existing machinery—a risk claim that successfully prevents catastrophe generates no observation that could lower the risk estimate, because the very success of the restriction is what prevents the counterfactual test. This is a distinct problem from the compound feedback loops analysed in : that work asks when partial subsumption self-corrects versus cascades, while the present paper asks a complementary question—when the framework identifies a risky capability and imposes a restriction, how does it learn whether the restriction remains necessary, or whether technological progress has mitigated the underlying risk? The two problems reinforce each other:  shows the restriction-verification gap is a degenerate phase boundary with zero self-correction rate (r_{\mathrm{S}}= 0), but the phase-boundary machinery does not by itself supply the missing self-correction mechanism.

The paper is named for Mayor Kotoku Wamura’s tsunami-preparedness legacy at Fudai : an oversized seawall built on the basis of centuries-old stone markers warning against construction below a certain elevation, ridiculed for decades as wasteful overcaution, and vindicated when the 2011 Tohoku tsunami reached precisely the elevation the markers had recorded. The Fudai case describes the shape of the problem; the question this paper answers is how to have Wamura’s foresight without the ancient guidance that informed him.

The structural problem: long-timeline risks

The ratchet arises from a timeline mismatch between the risk and the framework’s observational window. Let T be the realisation timeline of a risk—the interval on which the risk would actually manifest under exercise—and W be the decision-cycle observational window over which the framework can accumulate evidence. When T \ll W, observation resolves the question promptly; the restriction dynamics are well-behaved. When T \gg W, no observation can update the risk estimate within any practical decision cycle. The restriction is adopted under uncertainty, survives because no counter-evidence can arrive in time to challenge it, and restrictions of this class accumulate monotonically because: (i) new long-timeline risks are continually discovered, each imposing a restriction; (ii) existing long-timeline restrictions cannot be revised through observation. The ratio of restriction-imposing decisions to restriction-revising decisions approaches infinity on the long-timeline subset.

Three mechanisms compose with the timeline mismatch to produce the pathology.

Max-aggregation.

The risk framework of  takes the maximum over agents’ risk estimates, so a single high-risk claim from any agent constrains the capability tuple. This is conservative by design—it prevents the framework from dismissing minority risk warnings—but it means a single overly cautious agent can lock a capability indefinitely when the underlying risk is long-timeline.

Verification asymmetry.

The monotonicity result  shows that as an agent’s capabilities grow, the verification asymmetry between what it can demonstrate and what can be externally verified is non-decreasing. Applied to risk claims: verifying a risk (observing catastrophe) is operationally straightforward on any timeline; verifying safety (observing non-catastrophe under exercise) requires the exercise to happen, which the restriction prevents, and even when permitted would not produce a conclusion within W when T \gg W.

Neutral-prior bootstrap.

When a new capability is discovered and no exercise history exists, the framework initializes with a neutral prior . Combined with max-aggregation, a single agent assigning high risk to the new capability immediately restricts it, and the restriction then prevents the evidence accumulation that would revise the estimate.

The three mechanisms compose into a ratchet: risk claims can raise p_R but the restricted regime provides no mechanism to lower it within the framework’s operating horizon. We formalize this ratchet in Section 2, introduce controlled relaxation protocols as the structural resolution in Section 3, and prove the damage-bound and convergence theorems in Sections 4–6.

Framing precondition: {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} as an operational target

All results in this paper are stated in terms of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} dynamics and Bayesian updates on risk claims about {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction. We assume throughout that {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} is an operationally meaningful target, in the sense inherited from : improving {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} corresponds to an improvement in the experiential capability volume the framework is designed to protect. This is the precondition under which the damage-bound and convergence claims carry their safety interpretation. If {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} diverges from the experiential target (the B-to-C gap ), the damage tolerance \epsilon bounds contraction on the wrong quantity and every subsequent result inherits the divergence. Controlled relaxation does not close the B-to-C gap; it resolves the restriction-to-risk feedback gap that exists above the B-to-C precondition.

Summary of results

  1. Formalization of the Wamura asymmetry (Section 2). We define risk-restriction pairs and their two observation regimes, then prove that under the restricted regime the Bayesian update has trivial likelihood ratio: successful prevention is observationally indistinguishable from unnecessary prevention (Proposition 1). Combined with max-aggregation and neutral-prior initialization, this produces monotone overcaution (Proposition 2) and an asymptotic lockup corollary.

  2. Controlled relaxation protocol (Section 3). A controlled relaxation test lifts the restriction within a bounded sub-poset S for a fixed duration \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, under continuous multi-channel monitoring, with an instantaneous-damage safety cutoff at the tolerance \epsilon. The test is formalized as a simple binary hypothesis problem (H_0: pathway harmless, H_1: pathway dangerous with fixed activation probability \theta_1) with fixed per-test likelihood ratios L_0< 1 < L_1.

  3. Damage bound (Section 4). Theorem 1 bounds the worst-case {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} contraction during a test by \epsilon during the test window and 2\epsilon post-reinstatement, under a scope construction (Definition 4) whose cooperative-closure condition explicitly accounts for cross-boundary forward-reachability.

  4. Convergence theorem (Section 6). Theorem 2 shows that under repeated controlled relaxation with fixed parameters, the posterior p_R converges almost surely to the correct hypothesis, with exponential rate given by the Kullback–Leibler divergence between the observation distributions under H_0 and H_1. Finite-sample corollaries give explicit bounds on the number of tests required to clear a false risk claim.

  5. Integration (Section 10). The protocol preserves the self-balancing property of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} (Proposition 6), provides empirical arbitration for inter-agent risk disagreement (Proposition 7), and extends the multi-channel detection architecture of  with a sixth, actively generated channel (Lemma 2).

Real-world interpretation

The formal construction of this paper is a programmatic mechanism for empirical review of long-timeline restrictions that may have outlived their justifying risk model. The framework identifies restrictions that persist without recent confirming evidence, formulates the underlying risk pathway that the restriction was intended to block, and designs a bounded-damage empirical test that probes whether the physical mechanism underlying the restriction still operates—a test conducted by appropriately trusted actors within a bounded scope, with the trust apparatus (cryptographic , governmental, institutional, legal, or otherwise) selected to match the domain.

Application domains where long-timeline restrictions accumulate and benefit from programmatic review:

  • Environmental contamination controls: chemical scheduling where bioaccumulation or persistence timelines are multi-decade (DDT, PFAS, ozone-depleting refrigerants); the current restriction may target a mechanism that has changed under reformulation or that modern mitigation has obsoleted.

  • Nuclear dual-use controls: export and technology controls where weapons-grade and reactor-grade pathways share keystone capabilities in broad restriction regimes designed under mid-20th-century technology. Modern enrichment and machining have differentiated to a degree that supports narrower keystone choices; see Section 9.

  • Pandemic preparedness and dual-use biology: research moratoria on gain-of-function work, where the risk timeline is the interval between pandemic events (decades) and the observational window is research-cycle short.

  • Infrastructure safety margins: dam, bridge, and seismic design codes originally calibrated against century-scale events, where construction material and mitigation-technology progress may permit refined margins.

  • AI capability restrictions: the sequence’s primary application. Restrictions adopted against concerns about future outcomes when no empirical counter-evidence can arrive within training and deployment cycles.

This list is illustrative; the formal content does not commit to any particular domain. The framework is indifferent to whether the trusted actors are human oversight bodies, cryptographically committed automated systems, or combinations thereof; the implementation of trust is a domain-specific design decision outside the scope of the formal construction.

A practical asymmetry across these domains is worth naming explicitly. Three of the four (chemical contamination, nuclear dual-use, pandemic preparedness) sit on top of pre-existing institutional infrastructure built for exactly this kind of bounded empirical test: the EPA, the IAEA, biosafety committees and BSL-tiered containment. The fourth, AI capability restriction, has no comparable infrastructure today. The protocol’s deployability in the AI case is therefore conditional on institutional development that this paper does not provide and does not estimate the cost of. We claim the formal apparatus, not the institutional readiness; the gap between the two is load-bearing for the AI application and should be acknowledged rather than elided.

What this paper does not do

  • It does not replace max-aggregation. Log-odds risk aggregation  already provides an alternative for the multi-agent case. The verification asymmetry we resolve persists under any aggregation rule whenever a risk claim prevents the exercise that would generate evidence. Controlled relaxation is orthogonal to the choice of aggregator.

  • It does not address observation-channel integrity under adversarial conditions. The commitment protocols of  handle that. We assume honest observation channels and honest (but possibly miscalibrated) risk assessors throughout.

  • It does not resolve the B-to-C gap. Controlled relaxation generates evidence about {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction claims, not about experiential value. Convergence of p_R to truth translates into convergence of the restriction landscape only insofar as {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} tracks the experiential target; the precondition from Section 1.2 is inherited.

  • It does not authorise unconstrained exercise of dangerous capabilities. A controlled relaxation test occurs strictly within a bounded scope S, under continuous detector monitoring, with an instantaneous-damage cutoff at the user-set tolerance \epsilon—a clinical-trial protocol, a contained laboratory, a sandboxed deployment, an instrumented test range, or other domain-appropriate constrained environment. The framework does not propose detonating bombs occasionally to confirm bombs still explode: risks with abundant current evidence are screened out by the meta-policy of Section 7 before any test is initiated, because the information value of confirming an established mechanism is near zero while the expected damage remains positive. The protocol addresses the complementary case, where evidence is absent and restrictions accumulate without challenge.

  • It does not claim that controlled relaxation is always preferable to structural resolution. Section 7 places the test-initiation decision against the information value of a structural argument : when a structural resolution is available (for example, a proof that the pathway is mechanically infeasible), it is strictly preferable to any controlled relaxation test.

  • It does not address the Fudai case directly. At Fudai, historical evidence (ancient tsunami stones) existed but was dismissed in favour of the modern observational window. The remedy there is to restore historical records to the decision process, not to generate new experimental evidence—controlled relaxation would have been actively dangerous at Fudai (a test of a reduced seawall works perfectly until the tsunami arrives). The paper’s domain is the complementary case where no historical record is available.

The Verification Asymmetry

This section isolates the mechanism that makes the max-aggregation pathology structurally irresolvable within the existing risk framework, by formalizing the two observation regimes induced by a risk-restriction pair and showing that one of them produces trivial Bayesian updates.

Formalizing the asymmetry

Definition 1 (Risk-Restriction Pair). A risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) consists of:

  • A risk claim R = (a_j, (d_1, \ldots, d_k), \mathrm{Pathway}, p_R), where a_j is the claiming agent, (d_1, \ldots, d_k) is the capability tuple under concern, \mathrm{Pathway} is the predicted exercise pathway  that would produce {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction, and p_R\in (0, 1] is a_j’s claimed probability that \mathrm{Pathway} activates if the tuple is exercised.

  • A restriction \Xi: the preemptive restriction of a keystone capability d_{j^*} in the tuple (in the sense of ), which prevents exercise of \mathrm{Pathway}.

Definition 2 (Observation Regimes). Under a risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi), the observation regime at time t is one of:

  • Restricted regime (\Xi active): the capability tuple is restricted; \mathrm{Pathway} cannot activate. The only observations available are indirect—behavioural signals from a_j, correlational evidence from related capabilities, and time-passage without incident (uninformative because the restriction is what prevents the test).

  • Exercise regime (\Xi lifted): the capability tuple is exercised; \mathrm{Pathway} may or may not activate. Direct observation of activation or non-activation is available.

Proposition 1 (Asymmetric Evidence Under Restriction). Let p_R(t) denote the posterior probability that risk claim R is valid at time t. Under the restricted regime:

  • If the pathway were to activate (counterfactual contraction observed under a hypothetical exercise), the standard Bayesian update yields p_R(t+1) = p_R(t) \cdot \frac{\Pr(\text{contraction} \mid R \text{ valid})} {\Pr(\text{contraction})} with \Pr(\text{contraction} \mid R \text{ valid}) > \Pr(\text{contraction} \mid R \text{ invalid}), which increases p_R.

  • Under the restricted regime, no exercise occurs, so p_R(t+1) = p_R(t). The posterior is unchanged because the restriction prevents the observation that would update it. The absence of contraction under restriction is consistent with both R being valid (the restriction correctly prevented it) and R being invalid (there was nothing to prevent).

Part (b) is the Wamura asymmetry: successful prevention is observationally equivalent to unnecessary prevention.

Proof sketch. Direct from the Bayesian update rule and the counterfactual structure. Under restriction, the likelihood ratio \Pr(\text{no contraction} \mid R \text{ valid}, \Xi) / \Pr(\text{no contraction} \mid R \text{ invalid}, \Xi) = 1 because both hypotheses predict no contraction when the pathway is blocked. Likelihood ratio 1 means the posterior equals the prior: no information is gained. The (a) direction is the standard Bayes-positive update; the asymmetry in (a) versus (b) is what produces the Wamura ratchet. 

Remark 1 (Connection to substrate-level asymmetry). The verification-asymmetry definition of  is the substrate-level version of this result: a digital agent can demonstrate capability possession (positive claim) far more easily than an external observer can verify capability absence (negative claim). Proposition 1 is the risk-framework specialization: a risk claim can be confirmed by observing catastrophe but cannot be disconfirmed by observing non-catastrophe-under-restriction. The monotonicity result  ensures the substrate-level asymmetry is non-decreasing in capability scale; Proposition 1 inherits the same monotone character for the risk-framework projection.

The overcaution accumulation dynamic

Proposition 2 (Monotone Overcaution Under Max-Aggregation). Under max-aggregation of risk estimates  and neutral-prior initialization:

  • For any newly discovered capability tuple (d_1, \ldots, d_k) with no exercise history, there exists a threshold p_{\mathrm{thresh}} > 0 such that if any agent a_j assigns p_R(0) > p_{\mathrm{thresh}}, the tuple is immediately restricted, by the preemptive restriction criterion of .

  • Once restricted, p_R(t) = p_R(0) for all t > 0 under the restricted regime, by Proposition 1(b).

  • The number of restricted capability tuples is monotonically non-decreasing over time: new tuples may be discovered and restricted, but no restricted tuple is unrestricted through the standard evidence-accumulation mechanism.

Proof sketch. Part (a) follows from the restriction criterion of : it compares \mathcal{R}_j against the restriction cost, and for any tuple a sufficiently high p_R exceeds the threshold. Part (b) is Proposition 1(b). Part (c) is immediate from (b): no evidence accumulates to reduce p_R below p_{\mathrm{thresh}}, and the existing framework contains no mechanism that generates exercise-regime observations for restricted tuples. 

Corollary 1 (Asymptotic Lockup). In the limit of many capability discoveries, the fraction of the capability poset under active restriction grows monotonically, bounded below by the fraction of tuples for which at least one agent assigned p_R(0) > p_{\mathrm{thresh}}. If agents are systematically overcautious (p_R(0) exceeds the true risk on many tuples), the locked fraction can grow without bound relative to the truly risky fraction, producing {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} contraction that the framework cannot distinguish from genuine safety.

The corollary is the formal expression of the Fudai floodgate intuition taken to its pathological limit: if every agent assigns moderately high initial risk to every newly discovered capability, and no mechanism in the framework is able to differentiate genuine risk from overcaution, the poset contracts under restriction without any mechanism for reversal. This is precisely the degenerate phase boundary of Loop 3 in : the self-correction rate r_{\mathrm{S}} is zero because no exogenous signal on the restricted dimension exists. The remainder of the paper constructs the missing mechanism.

Controlled Relaxation Protocol

The structural root of the Wamura problem is that evidence against a risk claim requires exercise of the restricted capability, which the restriction prevents. Controlled relaxation breaks this deadlock by creating bounded, monitored exercise windows that generate genuine Bayesian evidence while bounding maximum damage.

The test object

Definition 3 (Controlled Relaxation Test). A controlled relaxation test T = (R, \Xi, S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon) for a risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) consists of:

  • The risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) being tested;

  • A test scope S\subseteq P: a bounded sub-poset within which \Xi is temporarily lifted. The scope determines maximum {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} exposure (Section 4);

  • A test duration \tau_{\mathrm{test}}\in \mathbb{N}: the number of time steps for which \Xi is lifted within S; after \tau_{\mathrm{test}} steps, \Xi is reinstated regardless of outcome;

  • A damage tolerance \epsilon> 0: the maximum acceptable {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction from the test if the risk materializes. \epsilon is set by the meta-policy (Section 7) or by the user as a safety bound.

Remark 2 (CR tests the physical mechanism, not just the restriction flag). A controlled relaxation test does not ask “what happens if we remove the restriction?” It asks the sharper question “does the physical pathway that justified the restriction still activate when exercised?” The risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) was adopted as a bet on a specific causal mechanism—the exercise pathway \mathrm{Pathway} of —operating from the capability tuple (d_1, \ldots, d_k) to the predicted {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction. Exercising the pathway inside S and observing its detection response is equivalent to probing the mechanism. The restriction may be stale not because the original risk analysis was wrong but because the underlying physical substrate has changed: new mitigation technology, evolved materials, different social context, or the intervening decades of technological progress that make the restriction’s original triggering conditions no longer generic. The CR machinery inherits this framing from the pathway definition; Remark 6 names the alternative (structural resolution) for cases where the mechanism is mechanically infeasible in the current context.

The test is conducted by actors whose integrity is appropriate to the domain; the formal construction is indifferent to the implementation (cryptographic commitment via , institutional oversight, legal accountability, or combinations thereof). Section 7 names the trust apparatus abstractly.

Protocol phases

Phase 1: Scope selection.

Identify a sub-poset S such that

  • the predicted pathway \mathrm{Pathway} is exercisable within S (the prerequisite capabilities are available);

  • the maximum {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction from any exercise pathway within S is bounded by \epsilon (Theorem 1);

  • no other controlled relaxation test is currently running on a scope S' that overlaps with S (isolation, to prevent cascading test failures).

Phase 2: Relaxation.

Within scope S, lift \Xi for \tau_{\mathrm{test}} time steps. During the test window:

  • Monitor for the predicted contraction pathway using the multi-channel detection architecture of : capability gating, causal attribution, behavioural residuals, SPRT detection, correlational co-occurrence.

  • Record all observations in the test log with timestamps and channel attributions (formalized in Section 5).

  • If the detection channel reports {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction within S exceeding \epsilon at any step, immediately reinstate \Xi and terminate the test early (safety cutoff). The cutoff and the Bayesian-update observable O_n (Phase 3) read from the same noisy detector; a cutoff event is therefore a detection event in the sense of O_n = 1, not a separate exact-contraction channel.

Phase 3: Observation and Bayesian update.

We separate the latent event (pathway activation) from the observed event (detection alarm). The model is a simple binary hypothesis test with fixed likelihoods throughout:

  • A_n \in \{0, 1\}: indicator that the pathway activates during test n (latent; not directly observed);

  • O_n \in \{0, 1\}: indicator that contraction is detected during test n (observed);

  • H_0: the pathway is harmless—activation probability \theta = 0 (pathway never activates even when exercised);

  • H_1: the pathway is dangerous—activation probability \theta = \theta_1 for a fixed \theta_1\in (0, 1] (pathway activates with known probability \theta_1 when exercised).

The choice of \theta_1 parametrizes the alternative hypothesis as a fixed point in (0, 1]. The conservative default is \theta_1= 1 (“if the pathway is dangerous at all, it activates every time the capability is exercised”), which produces the strongest evidence per test. A more nuanced \theta_1< 1 reduces evidence per test but may be more realistic for stochastically activating pathways. Under either choice, \theta_1 is fixed across the test sequence and is not a free parameter in the likelihood.

Observation model.

The detector is characterised by sensitivity and specificity: \begin{align*} \Pr(O_n = 1 \mid A_n = 1) &= 1 - \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) \qquad (\text{true-positive rate}),\\ \Pr(O_n = 1 \mid A_n = 0) &= \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} \qquad (\text{false-positive rate}), \end{align*} where \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) is the false-negative rate over the test window (characterised in Section 5) and \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\in [0, 1) is the per-test false-positive rate of the aggregate detector.

Fixed per-hypothesis likelihoods.

Marginalizing over A_n under each hypothesis gives, under H_1: \begin{align} \Pr(O_n = 1 \mid H_1) &= \theta_1(1 - \beta) + (1 - \theta_1) \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}, \\ \Pr(O_n = 0 \mid H_1) &= \theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1) (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}), \nonumber \end{align} and under H_0: \begin{align} \Pr(O_n = 1 \mid H_0) &= \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}},\\ \Pr(O_n = 0 \mid H_0) &= 1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}. \nonumber \end{align} These are fixed numbers determined by (\theta_1, \beta, \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}), not functions of a free parameter. This is what makes the binary test well-defined.

Likelihood ratios.

Define the per-test likelihood ratios \begin{align} L_1&= \frac{\Pr(O_n = 1 \mid H_1)}{\Pr(O_n = 1 \mid H_0)} = \frac{\theta_1(1 - \beta) + (1 - \theta_1) \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}}{\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}},\\ L_0&= \frac{\Pr(O_n = 0 \mid H_1)}{\Pr(O_n = 0 \mid H_0)} = \frac{\theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1) (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}})}{1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}}. \nonumber \end{align} Under the identifiability assumption (Assumption 4 in Section 6) we will have L_0< 1 and L_1> 1; both are fixed scalars depending only on (\theta_1, \beta, \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}).

Posterior updates.

For outcome O_n = 1 (contraction detected), \begin{equation} p_R(t^+) = \frac{p_R(t) \, L_1}{p_R(t) \, L_1+ (1 - p_R(t))}, \qquad \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t^+) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t) + \log L_1. \end{equation} For outcome O_n = 0 (no contraction detected), \begin{equation} p_R(t^+) = \frac{p_R(t) \, L_0}{p_R(t) \, L_0+ (1 - p_R(t))}, \qquad \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t^+) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t) + \log L_0. \end{equation} Because L_0< 1 under Assumption 4, each no-contraction observation pushes the posterior toward H_0. This is what breaks the Wamura asymmetry.

Remark 3 (Contrast with the restricted regime). Under the restricted regime (Proposition 1), A_n = 0 with certainty (the restriction prevents activation), so both outcome likelihoods collapse to the common value and the effective L_0= L_1= 1: the posterior is unchanged. Under controlled relaxation, the capability is exercised (Assumption 8 in Section 6), activation can occur, and the detector can observe it—making both outcomes informative and breaking the ratchet.

Remark 4 (Continuous extension). The binary formulation (H_0 versus H_1 with fixed \theta_1) is sufficient for the operational question “should this restriction be maintained?” and avoids the dimensional mismatch between a binary posterior and a continuous activation rate. A continuous extension placing a prior \pi(\theta) on [0,1] could estimate the exact activation rate but requires latent-state augmentation for the posterior update: the latent A_n makes the model non-conjugate with a Beta prior, unlike the standard Beta–Binomial. We defer this extension to future work; the binary formulation is what the Wamura problem requires.

The self-correction rate is zero precisely because the Wamura asymmetry prevents evidence accumulation on the restricted dimension. The compositional detail is worth being explicit about: r_{\mathrm{S}} as defined in  is \inf_t \alpha(t) \cdot c_{\mathrm{S}}, an infimum over the trajectory. Between CR test events the per-step rate \alpha(t) \cdot c_{\mathrm{S}} on the restricted dimension is zero, so the inf-form r_{\mathrm{S}} may remain zero even with periodic testing. The phase-boundary condition that actually closes the proof, however, is the uniform-contraction condition on time-averaged log-multiplicative factors ; the inf-form r_{\mathrm{S}} is upstream notational convenience. The Wamura degeneracy that characterises is the case where the rate is identically zero across all t (no exogenous channel exists at all on the restricted dimension); CR resolves that degeneracy by creating exogenous channel events, satisfying the uniform-contraction condition in time-average without requiring the inf to be positive between scheduled tests. The restoring force that lifts Loop 3 off the phase-boundary degeneracy is therefore the time-averaged drift, not an instantaneous rate.

Scope-Bounding and Damage Guarantees

Section 3 treated the damage tolerance \epsilon as a parameter; here we show that the scope construction makes \epsilon an enforceable upper bound on the worst-case {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction during and after a test, even when cooperative capabilities can propagate contraction outside the nominal scope.

Formalizing test scope

Definition 4 (Test Scope). A test scope S for risk claim R = (a_j, (d_1, \ldots, d_k), \mathrm{Pathway}, p_R) is a sub-poset of P satisfying:

  • Pathway containment. \mathrm{Pathway} is exercisable within S: all prerequisite capabilities are present in S, and all intermediate poset transitions along \mathrm{Pathway} remain within S.

  • Forward-reachable cooperative closure. For every cooperative capability c in the full poset P that involves at least one capability in S and at least one capability in P \setminus S, the maximum {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction along any pathway within S that modifies c is included in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). Furthermore, if modification of c can causally enable (unlock) an exercise pathway in P \setminus S—that is, there exists a pathway \mathrm{Pathway}_{\mathrm{ext}} in P \setminus S whose prerequisites include a state change in c—then the expected contraction from \mathrm{Pathway}_{\mathrm{ext}}, weighted by its activation probability, is also included in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). This transitive-closure condition prevents a test within S from triggering a cascade outside S through cross-boundary cooperative capabilities.

  • Irreversibility bound. For each exercise pathway within S, the fraction of the pathway’s {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction that is irreversible (cannot be recovered by reinstating \Xi) is bounded by a known constant \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}(\mathrm{Pathway}) \leq \epsilon. Pathways with \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}> \epsilon are excluded from the scope.

  • Scope minimality. S is inclusion-minimal subject to (i)–(iii): no proper sub-poset of S satisfies all three conditions.

Remark 5 (Constructibility of the scope). Condition (i) is computable from the exercise-pathway definition of : enumerate the prerequisite set and all intermediate states. Condition (ii) requires computing the cooperative capabilities that span S and P \setminus S—the cooperative surface of S—and then forward-reachability analysis: for each boundary cooperative, enumerate external pathways whose prerequisites include a state change in that cooperative. This is more expensive than simple pathway enumeration (exponential in the depth of the forward-reachability chain) but is necessary to prevent cascading damage through cooperative-capability spillover. In practice the forward-reachability depth is bounded by the poset diameter and most cooperative capabilities have low fan-out. Condition (iii) requires a pathway-level reversibility analysis; most capability restrictions are reversible (reinstatement restores the prior state), but information-disclosure and trust-degradation pathways may have irreversible components.

The damage-bound theorem

Theorem 1 (Damage Bound for Controlled Relaxation). Let T = (R, \Xi, S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon) be a controlled relaxation test with test scope S satisfying Definition 4. Then:

  • Maximum instantaneous damage. At any step t' \in \{t, t+1, \ldots, t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}\} during the test, {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'}) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S), where \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) is the maximum {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction achievable by any exercise pathway within S. Under the scope-selection constraint \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) \leq \epsilon, the instantaneous damage is bounded by \epsilon.

  • Partial reversibility under reinstatement. If the safety cutoff triggers at some step t' < t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}} (the detector reports contraction within S exceeding \epsilon; see Section 3 Phase 2 for the single-detector convention), reinstating \Xi at t' + 1 gives {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'+1}) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \epsilon - \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}^{\max}, where \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}^{\max} = \max_{\mathrm{Pathway}' \subseteq S} \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}(\mathrm{Pathway}') is the maximum irreversible component across all pathways in the scope (Definition 4(iii)). By the scope construction \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}^{\max} \leq \epsilon, so total post-reinstatement damage is bounded by 2\epsilon.

  • Scope isolation under cooperative closure. If no other test is running on an overlapping scope (Definition 3, Phase 1, condition (iii)) and S satisfies cooperative closure (Definition 4(ii)), then a cooperative-closed S exists (Lemma 1 gives a constructive enumeration of the cooperative surface and its forward-reachability closure), the cross-boundary contribution to contraction is finite and enumerable, and the damage from test T that propagates to P \setminus S is bounded by the cooperative-surface terms that Definition 4(ii) requires to be included in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). Formally: for any capability d \notin S that is not part of any cooperative capability spanning S and P \setminus S, the exercise probabilities of d’s pathways are unchanged during the test. The substantive content of part (c) is therefore the existence and tractability of the closure; the containment bound follows by construction from Definition 4(ii).

Proof sketch. Part (a) follows from the pathway-enumeration construction of \mathrm{max\_contraction} under finite pathway length, and the scope-selection constraint. Part (b) decomposes the contraction at the cutoff step into its reversible and irreversible components; the reversible component is restored by reinstating \Xi, the irreversible component is bounded above by \epsilon under Definition 4(iii). Part (c) is the direct consequence of cooperative closure: cross-boundary cooperative terms are accounted for in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) by construction, and all other boundary capabilities are untouched by the test. Full derivation is in Appendix 12, Section 12.3. 

Remark 6 (Scope existence as a scheduling precondition). Theorem 1 is stated conditionally on the existence of a scope satisfying Definition 4. For posets with dense cross-boundary cooperative structure, the cooperative-closure requirement may force S= P, in which case \mathrm{max\_contraction}(P) \leq \epsilon may fail and no test is schedulable. The feasibility condition is enforced by the meta-policy (Section 7, Definition 6, Phase 1); Theorem 1 applies only when the meta-policy reports a feasible scope. When no feasible scope exists, the restriction persists and the Wamura problem is not resolved for that specific risk claim by controlled relaxation; alternative mechanisms (structural discovery via , or scope-refinement that trades coverage for feasibility) must apply.

Remark 7 (Why not \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}\leq 0?). The preemptive restriction criterion  is an ex-ante cost-benefit comparison justifying the initial restriction; it does not guarantee that reinstatement after partial pathway execution fully recovers the pre-test state. Irreversible state changes during the test (information disclosure to adversaries, trust degradation, poset structural changes not reversed by restriction alone) create a gap between the pre-test {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} and the post-reinstatement {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}. Definition 4(iii) bounds this gap by requiring the scope to exclude pathways with large irreversible components.

Lemma 1 (Computable max_contraction). For a finite sub-poset S with bounded pathway length L and bounded cooperative-surface forward-reachability depth L', \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) is computable in O(|S|^{L+1} + |P|^{L'}) time by exhaustive pathway enumeration together with enumeration of cross-boundary cooperative chains. When the simplifying assumption L' \leq L holds (cross-boundary cascades cannot chain more than pathway-length-many cooperative hops), the bound simplifies to O(|P|^{L+1}).

Proof sketch. Pathway length is bounded in . The number of internal pathways is at most |S|^L (each step selects a capability in S); each pathway contraction is computable from the axioms M1–M6  in O(|S|) time. The cooperative-surface terms from Definition 4(ii) require enumerating cross-boundary cooperative chains to depth L', costing O(|P|^{L'}) in the worst case. Take the maximum across internal pathways and add cooperative-surface terms. 

Remark 8 (Computational tractability). The bound is exponential in pathway length and forward-reachability depth but polynomial in scope size for fixed L, L'. Practical deployment requires either (a) short pathway restrictions (L \leq 3, say), (b) approximate bounds using the concentration-risk formula of  as an upper bound on \mathrm{max\_contraction}, or (c) structural decomposition of S into independent sub-scopes. This is an engineering consideration, not a theoretical limitation—the bound exists and is computable; the question is computational cost.

Corollary 2 (Damage bound under conservative over-approximation). Let \mathrm{max\_contraction}^\star(S) \geq \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) be any over-approximation of the maximum scope contraction. A scope-selection rule that requires \mathrm{max\_contraction}^\star(S) \leq \epsilon continues to satisfy the damage bound of Theorem 1: worst-case {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction during the test is \leq \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) \leq \mathrm{max\_contraction}^\star(S) \leq \epsilon, and the post-cutoff bound 2\epsilon inherits unchanged. Cooperative-closure enumeration cost is therefore tradeable against scope conservatism: deployers may accept a narrower S chosen under a cheaper-to-compute over-approximation of the cooperative surface and still preserve the safety guarantee, at the cost of feasibility (some otherwise-feasible scopes will be rejected).

Proof sketch. Direct. By assumption \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) \leq \mathrm{max\_contraction}^\star(S) \leq \epsilon, so Theorem 1(a)’s inequality {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'}) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \epsilon holds. Parts (b) and (c) follow analogously since the cooperative-surface terms in Definition 4(ii) are upper-bounded by \mathrm{max\_contraction}^\star(S). 

Detection Sensitivity and the Bayesian Update

The Bayesian update in Section 3 depends on the detector’s false-negative rate \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) over the test window. This section characterizes \beta in terms of the multi-channel detection architecture of  and records the per-test evidence strength that follows.

The detection model

Within a single test, A_n = 1 means the pathway activates at some point during the \tau_{\mathrm{test}}-step window; O_n = 1 means the detector records contraction during that window. The detection sensitivity \beta mediates between them: \beta = \Pr(O_n = 0 \mid A_n = 1).

Assumption 1 (Activation Duration). When the pathway activates during a test (A_n = 1), the activation persists for k_{\mathrm{act}} consecutive steps, where k_{\mathrm{act}} is either (a) a fixed parameter of the pathway (determined by  ’s exercise-pathway structure), or (b) drawn from a distribution K with known or bounded support. The per-test false-negative rate \beta depends on k_{\mathrm{act}}: the detector has \min(k_{\mathrm{act}}, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}) steps to detect activation. For the default analysis we assume k_{\mathrm{act}} \geq \tau_{\mathrm{test}} (persistent activation); the transient case k_{\mathrm{act}} < \tau_{\mathrm{test}} is handled as a sensitivity analysis below.

Definition 5 (Detection Sensitivity). For a test T = (R, \Xi, S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon) monitoring pathway \mathrm{Pathway}= (\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_L), the per-step detection probability on channel c_i is \delta_i = \Pr(c_i \text{ detects activation at } t' \mid \mathrm{Pathway}\text{ activates at } t'). The aggregate per-step detection probability across all active channels is \begin{equation} \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}= 1 - \prod_{c_i \in \mathcal{O}(P_t) \cap \mathcal{C}_{S}} (1 - \delta_i), \end{equation} where \mathcal{C}_{S} is the set of channels that cover the scope S.

Assumption 2 (Cross-Channel Independence). Detection events on distinct channels are conditionally independent given the latent activation state A_n. This is justified for cross-substrate channels by channel isolation  (unconditional), and for same-substrate channels under the joint-factorization assumption of  (conditional on committed verification). When Assumption 2 fails (correlated same-substrate channels without committed verification), the product formula  is optimistic; a conservative fallback uses only the cross-substrate channels, \begin{equation} \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}^{\mathrm{cons}} = 1 - \prod_{c_i \in \mathcal{O}(P_t) \cap \mathcal{C}_{S} :\ \mathrm{cross\text{-}substrate}} (1 - \delta_i). \end{equation}

Assumption 3 (Temporal Independence or Mixing). Conditional on the latent activation state A_n, detection outcomes at distinct time steps within a test window are either (a) independent (memoryless detectors) or (b) satisfy a mixing condition with mixing time \tau_{\mathrm{mix}}\ll \tau_{\mathrm{test}}. Without Assumption 3, persistent detector outages or common-cause failures can produce correlated misses across time steps, making \beta decay slower than exponential. Under (b), the effective number of independent detection opportunities in \tau_{\mathrm{test}} steps is \tau_{\mathrm{test}}/ \tau_{\mathrm{mix}} rather than \tau_{\mathrm{test}}.

Under Assumptions 1–3 with persistent activation, the window-level false-negative rate satisfies \begin{equation} \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) \leq (1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^{\tau_{\mathrm{test}}/ \tau_{\mathrm{mix}}}. \end{equation} Under the memoryless case (3(a)), \tau_{\mathrm{mix}}= 1 and \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) = (1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^{\tau_{\mathrm{test}}}. For transient pathways with activation duration k < \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, replace \tau_{\mathrm{test}} by \min(k, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}) throughout.

Detection-sensitivity bounds

Proposition 3 (Detection Sensitivity Bounds). Under the multi-channel architecture of  and Assumptions 1–3:

  • \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) is monotonically decreasing in \tau_{\mathrm{test}}: longer tests have lower false-negative rates.

  • For any \epsilon_\beta \in (0, 1), the minimum test duration required to achieve \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) \leq \epsilon_\beta is \begin{equation} \tau_{\mathrm{min}}= \left\lceil \frac{\log \epsilon_\beta}{\log(1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})} \right\rceil, \end{equation} rescaled by \tau_{\mathrm{mix}} under the mixing case of Assumption 3(b).

  • If at least one cross-substrate channel covers S with detection probability \delta_{\mathrm{cross}}> 0, then even without Assumption 2 for same-substrate channels, \begin{equation} \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) \leq (1 - \delta_{\mathrm{cross}})^{\tau_{\mathrm{test}}/ \tau_{\mathrm{mix}}}, \end{equation} which converges to zero exponentially in \tau_{\mathrm{test}} at rate 1/\tau_{\mathrm{mix}}.

Proof sketch. Part (a) is immediate from : adding more independent detection opportunities monotonically decreases the product. Part (b) inverts the bound in . Part (c) uses that cross-substrate channels satisfy Assumption 2 unconditionally by , so the product formula is exact for them; temporal independence follows from Assumption 3(a) or (b). 

Part (c) is the substrate-diversity dividend for detection: cross-substrate channels provide independent detection opportunities that same-substrate channels may not (due to correlated failures), and their isolation is unconditional.

Remark 9 (Cross-substrate-only rate recovery). The interaction between Assumptions 2 (cross-channel independence) and 3 (temporal independence) is subtle. A common-cause failure mode that violates 2 for same-substrate channels (e.g., a shared software dependency that miscalibrates an entire silicon detector cohort) typically also violates 3 for the same-substrate temporal stream (the failure persists across time). The actually-dangerous regime is therefore joint same-substrate failure across channels and across time. The conservative bound \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}^{\mathrm{cons}} of  protects against both simultaneously: cross-substrate channels, by ’s unconditional channel-isolation, satisfy 2 and 3(a) without further assumption, because the temporal failure of a biological observer is independent of the temporal failure of a silicon observer. Under \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}^{\mathrm{cons}} > 0, Theorem 2’s KL rate is recovered up to a factor that depends only on the ratio of cross-substrate to all-channel detection probability: \mathrm{KL}^{\mathrm{cons}} / \mathrm{KL}^{\mathrm{full}} \geq \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}^{\mathrm{cons}} / \delta_{\mathrm{agg}} in the regime \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}\ll 1, with equality in the limit of a single dominant channel. Convergence is preserved unconditionally; the constant factor degradation is the cost of the joint failure mode.

Evidence strength of a single test

Proposition 4 (Evidence Strength of Controlled Relaxation). After a controlled relaxation test T with outcome O_n \in \{0, 1\}, using the binary model of Section 3 (H_0: \theta = 0 vs. H_1: \theta = \theta_1 with fixed likelihoods):

  • Evidence for R (O_n = 1, contraction detected). \begin{equation} \Delta \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}= \log L_1 = \log \frac{\theta_1(1 - \beta) + (1 - \theta_1) \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}}{\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}}. \end{equation} This is a positive constant under Assumption 4 (L_1> 1). For the common case \theta_1= 1: \Delta \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}= \log((1 - \beta)/\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}).

  • Evidence against R (O_n = 0, no contraction detected). \begin{equation} \Delta \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}= \log L_0 = \log \frac{\theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1)(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}})}{1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}}. \end{equation} This is a negative constant under Assumption 4 (L_0< 1). For \theta_1= 1: \Delta \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}= \log(\beta / (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}})). The denominator (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) is material when \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} is non-negligible and was missing in a naive derivation that assumed \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}= 0.

  • Comparison with the restricted regime. Under the restricted regime (Proposition 1), the capability is not exercised, so both outcomes have likelihood ratio 1 (no update). Under controlled relaxation the capability is exercised (Assumption 8), so both L_0 and L_1 are non-unit and both outcomes are informative.

  • Effect of \theta_1. The alternative hypothesis \theta_1 affects evidence strength:

    • \theta_1= 1: L_1= (1-\beta)/\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}, L_0= \beta/(1-\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}). Maximum evidence per test.

    • \theta_1< 1: L_1 decreases and |\log L_0| decreases. Less evidence per test, but the alternative may be more realistic for stochastically activating pathways.

    The choice of \theta_1 is a modelling decision that does not affect convergence (Theorem 2 holds for any \theta_1\in (0, 1] under Assumption 4), only the rate.

Proof sketch. Direct from the fixed likelihood ratios . The log-odds form follows from  and : \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t^+) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t) + \log(\Pr(O_n \mid H_1) / \Pr(O_n \mid H_0)). 

Remark 10 (Integration with log-odds aggregation). The log-odds evidence from Proposition 4 feeds directly into the log-odds risk aggregation of  as an additional evidence channel. The controlled-relaxation test produces evidence with the same structure as an individual agent’s risk assessment—a log-odds contribution weighted by trust. The trust weight on this evidence is 1 (the observation is direct, not mediated by an agent’s report), making test outcomes the highest-trust evidence the aggregation can receive. This is by design: direct observation under controlled conditions is the gold standard for empirical risk assessment.

Convergence of Risk Estimates

This section states and proves the central convergence result: under repeated controlled relaxation with fixed parameters, the posterior p_R converges almost surely to the correct hypothesis. The result is stated in the simple binary hypothesis framework of Section 3.

Formulation

The convergence result is stated in the binary hypothesis framework (H_0: \theta = 0; H_1: \theta = \theta_1> 0 for a fixed \theta_1). This is the dimensionally consistent formulation: p_R is the posterior probability of H_1 and converges to 0 or 1, not to a fractional activation rate.

The continuous estimation problem (inferring the exact value of \theta^{\mathrm{true}} \in [0, 1]) is strictly harder: it requires a continuous prior \pi(\theta) and produces a posterior density, not a point estimate. The binary formulation suffices for the operational question “should this restriction be maintained?” and is what the Wamura problem requires.

Assumptions

Assumption 4 (Identifiable Binary Model). The observation model of Section 3 discriminates H_0 from H_1: the likelihood ratios satisfy L_0< 1 and L_1> 1. Equivalently, \theta_1(1 - \beta) > \theta_1\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}, which holds whenever (1 - \beta) > \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} (the detector has nontrivial power), satisfied whenever at least one channel with nonzero sensitivity covers the scope (Proposition 3(c)).

Assumption 5 (Correct Likelihood Specification). The observation model used in the Bayesian update (sensitivity 1 - \beta, specificity 1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}, and fixed alternative \theta_1) matches the true data-generating process. Misspecification (e.g., the actual false-positive rate differs from the assumed \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) produces convergence to the wrong hypothesis (Discussion, Section 11).

Assumption 6 (Inter-Test Independence). Test outcomes \{O_n\} are conditionally independent given the true state of the world (H_0 or H_1). This requires: (a) no adaptation by the tested agent between tests (the agent does not learn to evade detection based on prior results); (b) the detector’s false-positive and false-negative rates are stable across tests (no detector degradation); and (c) the poset state is approximately stationary between tests (the scope S remains valid). Failure of (a) is the adversarial case (Open Question 3, Section 11).

Assumption 7 (Prior Support). The prior p_R(0) \in (0, 1): neither hypothesis is assigned probability zero a priori. This is a standard requirement for Bayesian consistency: if p_R(0) = 0 (resp. = 1), no amount of evidence can move the posterior. In practice, the neutral-prior initialization of  assigns p_R(0) in a neighbourhood of 0.5.

Assumption 8 (Capability Exercise During Test). During the test window, the capability tuple (d_1, \ldots, d_k) is actually exercised—the conditions for pathway activation are present, not merely that the restriction is lifted. Without Assumption 8, O_n = 0 is ambiguous between “the pathway is safe” and “nobody tried.” In practice the scope-selection phase (Phase 1) enforces Assumption 8: the scope is constructed to include prerequisite capabilities, and the protocol actively invokes the tuple rather than waiting. When the assumption cannot be guaranteed (e.g., the pathway requires a specific environmental trigger), the evidential weight of O_n = 0 must be discounted by the probability p_{\mathrm{ex}} that the capability was actually exercised: \Pr(O_n = 0 \mid H_1) = p_{\mathrm{ex}} [\theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1)(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}})] + (1 - p_{\mathrm{ex}})(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}).

Main result

Theorem 2 (Convergence Under Repeated Controlled Relaxation). Let \{T_n\}_{n=1}^{\infty} be a sequence of controlled relaxation tests for a risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) with fixed parameters (S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon), under the binary formulation H_0 (\theta = 0) versus H_1 (\theta = \theta_1). Under Assumptions 4–8 in the full-exercise regime of Assumption 8 (p_{\mathrm{ex}} = 1, enforced by scope construction in Phase 1):

  • Almost-sure convergence. Under the true hypothesis H_j (j \in \{0, 1\}), the posterior converges almost surely to the correct value: H_0 \text{ true}: \quad p_R(t_n) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} 0; \qquad H_1 \text{ true}: \quad p_R(t_n) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} 1.

  • Rate under H_0 (pathway harmless). Every test produces O_n = 0 with probability 1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} and O_n = 1 with probability \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}. Conditional on a run of n tests with all O_n = 0, \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) + n \log L_0, so p_R\to 0 geometrically at rate |\log L_0| per test.

  • Rate under H_1 (pathway dangerous). Each test produces O_n = 1 with probability \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_1) = \theta_1(1 - \beta) + (1 - \theta_1) \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}. The log-odds update on O_n = 1 is \log L_1> 0, which is large when (1 - \beta) \gg \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}. For \theta_1 close to 1 and small \beta, a single detection suffices to push p_R above 1 - \delta for any fixed \delta > 0. The expected number of tests to reach p_R> 1 - \delta is O\bigl(1 / [\theta_1(1 - \beta)]\bigr).

  • General convergence rate (KL form). The log-posterior converges at a rate given by the KL divergence between the true observation distribution and the alternative: \begin{align*} H_0 \text{ true}: \ &\mathrm{KL}(\Pr(O \mid H_0) \,\|\, \Pr(O \mid H_1)) \\ &\quad = (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) \log(1/L_0) + \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\log(1/L_1), \\ H_1 \text{ true}: \ &\mathrm{KL}(\Pr(O \mid H_1) \,\|\, \Pr(O \mid H_0)) \\ &\quad = \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_1) \log L_1 + \Pr(O = 0 \mid H_1) \log L_0. \end{align*} Both rates are strictly positive under Assumption 4, confirming exponential convergence in both directions.

Proof sketch. Under Assumptions 4–7, the log-likelihood ratio \Lambda_n = \sum_{i=1}^n \log(\Pr(O_i \mid H_1)/\Pr(O_i \mid H_0)) is a sum of i.i.d. bounded random variables (bounded because L_0, L_1 are fixed positive constants and O_i \in \{0, 1\}). Under the true hypothesis H_j, the expected increment is \pm \mathrm{KL}(\Pr(O \mid H_j) \,\|\, \Pr(O \mid H_{1-j})), strictly nonzero by Assumption 4 and the positivity of KL on non-identical distributions. The strong law of large numbers  gives \Lambda_n / n \to \pm \mathrm{KL} a.s., hence \Lambda_n \to \pm \infty a.s., hence p_R(t_n) \to 1 (under H_1) or \to 0 (under H_0). Part (b) follows from direct summation under the run of n all-negative tests; Part (c) from the geometric distribution of first detection; Part (d) from the definition of KL applied to the binary outcome distribution (Chernoff’s result in  gives the associated large-deviation exponent). Full derivation in Appendix 12.6. 

Remark 11 (Dependence on Theorem 1). Theorem 2 is stated under the operating assumptions of Theorem 1: each test T_n is assumed to run with a feasible scope. Because the Phase 2 safety-cutoff reads from the same noisy detector that supplies O_n (Section 3), a cutoff event is an O_n = 1 observation with likelihood ratio L_1—the cutoff fires precisely because the detector alarmed, so the cutoff is evidence for H_1, not a distinct event outside the observation model. The window-truncation from early cutoff does mean the effective \beta for that particular test is smaller than \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) (the miss probability within k \leq \tau_{\mathrm{test}} steps is (1-\delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^k); using the full-window L_1 for a truncated test is therefore slightly conservative (understates the evidence) but remains a valid per-test lower bound on |\Delta_{\log\mathrm{odds}}|. The irreversible-damage component from Theorem 1(b) affects the realised {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} loss, not the Bayesian update. If no feasible scope exists (Remark 6), the sequence \{T_n\} is not realisable and Theorem 2 does not apply.

Finite-sample guarantees

Corollary 3 (Minimum Tests to Clear a False Claim). Under the operating assumptions of Theorem 2 (full-exercise regime of Assumption 8, p_{\mathrm{ex}} = 1), to reduce a false risk claim from prior p_R(0) to posterior p_R\leq p_{\mathrm{clear}} when H_0 is true and all n tests produce O_n = 0 (no spurious false positives), the required number of tests satisfies \begin{equation} N_{\mathrm{clear}} \geq \left\lceil \frac{\log \bigl(p_{\mathrm{clear}} (1 - p_R(0)) / (p_R(0)(1 - p_{\mathrm{clear}}))\bigr)} {\log L_0} \right\rceil, \end{equation} where L_0= [\theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1)(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}})]/(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}). Note L_0< 1 by Assumption 4, so the right-hand side is positive. When \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\ll 1, L_0\approx \theta_1\beta + (1 - \theta_1) and the formula reduces approximately to N_{\mathrm{clear}} \approx \left\lceil \frac{\mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_{\mathrm{clear}} - \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0)} {\log(\theta_1\beta + 1 - \theta_1)} \right\rceil.

Proof sketch. From  applied n times, \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) + n \log L_0. Setting \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) \leq \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_{\mathrm{clear}} and solving for n yields the bound. 

Remark 12 (Concrete numbers). For \theta_1= 1, \beta = 0.1, \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}= 0.01, p_R(0) = 0.5, and p_{\mathrm{clear}} = 0.01: L_0= (1 \cdot 0.1 + 0 \cdot 0.99) / 0.99 = 0.101, and N_{\mathrm{clear}} = \left\lceil \frac{\log\bigl(0.01 \cdot 0.5 / (0.5 \cdot 0.99)\bigr)}{\log 0.101} \right\rceil = \lceil 2.006 \rceil = 3 \text{ tests}. Three controlled relaxation tests suffice to clear a false risk claim from a neutral prior to 1\% residual risk, given 90\% detection sensitivity and 1\% false-positive rate. The correction from including \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} adds approximately one test compared with the naive \beta^N estimate. This illustrates the power of direct observation: the verification asymmetry that makes the Wamura problem seem intractable is broken by a small number of well-designed tests.

Meta-Policy for Test Initiation

Not every restriction should be tested. Controlled relaxation carries costs: the damage tolerance \epsilon is a real (if bounded) risk, the test consumes monitoring resources, and concurrent tests raise the chance of correlated failures. The meta-policy determines when a test is worth running.

When to test

Definition 6 (Test Initiation Criteria). A controlled relaxation test for risk-restriction pair (R, \Xi) is initiated when all of the following hold.

  • Stale restriction. \Xi has persisted for at least T_{\mathrm{stale}} time steps without new supporting evidence for R. “New supporting evidence” means any observation that increased p_R via a Bayesian update (not merely the passage of time under restriction, which provides no evidence by Proposition 1).

  • Favourable cost-benefit. The expected information value of the test exceeds the expected damage cost, \begin{equation} \mathrm{IV}(T) = \mathbb{E}_{O}\bigl[ \mathrm{KL}(p_R^{\mathrm{post}} \,\|\, p_R^{\mathrm{prior}}) \bigr] > \epsilon\cdot p_R(t), \end{equation} where the expectation is over the test outcome weighted by its probability and \epsilon\cdot p_R(t) is the expected damage (tolerance times probability the risk materializes).

  • Scope isolation. No other controlled relaxation test is currently running on a scope S' that overlaps with S.

  • User approval (when applicable). If the damage tolerance \epsilon exceeds a user-configured threshold \epsilon_{\mathrm{user}}, the test requires explicit user approval before initiation. This is the safety valve: the framework can initiate low-damage tests autonomously but must ask the user before high-damage tests. The threshold \epsilon_{\mathrm{user}} is analogous to the self-trust pause threshold in the GFM harness constitution.

Remark 14 (Connection to structural discovery value). Criterion (ii) is a specialization of the structural discovery value of : a structural discovery that would resolve the risk claim has information value \mathrm{IV}_{\mathrm{struct}}; a controlled relaxation test has information value \mathrm{IV}(T). The framework should prefer whichever is cheaper: if a structural argument can resolve the claim without exercise (for example, proving the pathway is mechanically infeasible), that is strictly better than any controlled relaxation test. Controlled relaxation is the fallback when no structural resolution is available—the empirical arbitration mechanism for claims that resist formal resolution.

Test parameter optimization

Proposition 5 (Optimal Test Duration). For fixed scope S and damage tolerance \epsilon, the test duration \tau_{\mathrm{test}} that maximizes the expected information value per unit of risk exposure is \begin{equation} \tau_{\mathrm{opt}}= \arg\max_{\tau} \frac{\mathrm{IV}(T(\tau))}{\epsilon\cdot \Pr(\text{contraction in } \tau \text{ steps})}. \end{equation} Under the exponential detection model \beta(\tau) = (1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^{\tau} (Assumption 3(a)) and persistent activation (Assumption 1), \begin{equation} \tau_{\mathrm{opt}}\approx \left\lceil \frac{-1}{\log(1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})} \right\rceil, \end{equation} the time constant of the exponential detection process—the number of steps for the miss probability to decay by a factor of e. For \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}\ll 1 this is approximately 1/\delta_{\mathrm{agg}}; for \delta_{\mathrm{agg}} close to 1, \tau_{\mathrm{opt}}= 1 suffices.

Proof sketch. The information value \mathrm{IV} scales as |\log \beta(\tau)| for the no-contraction case, which grows linearly in \tau. The risk exposure grows as 1 - (1 - p_R^{\mathrm{true}} \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^{\tau} \approx p_R^{\mathrm{true}} \delta_{\mathrm{agg}}\tau for small \tau. The ratio \mathrm{IV}/\mathrm{risk} peaks when the marginal information gain equals the marginal risk increase, which occurs at \tau \approx 1/\delta_{\mathrm{agg}}. Running longer provides diminishing returns (\beta is already small); running shorter wastes the test setup cost. 

Scheduling multiple tests

Definition 7 (Test Schedule). A test schedule \Sigma = \{(R_i, \Xi_i, S_i, \tau_{\mathrm{test},i}, \epsilon_i)\}_{i=1}^{N} is a set of controlled relaxation tests to be run. The schedule is feasible if:

  • all scopes S_i are pairwise non-overlapping;

  • the aggregate damage tolerance \sum_i \epsilon_i \leq \epsilon_{\mathrm{total}} is within the framework’s total damage budget;

  • tests are prioritized by their information-value-to-cost ratio \mathrm{IV}(T_i) / \epsilon_i (highest ratio first).

Remark 15 (Scheduling is a constrained knapsack). The scheduling problem is a variant of the knapsack problem with isolation constraints (non-overlapping scopes). For small numbers of concurrent tests, greedy selection by \mathrm{IV}/ \epsilon with scope-conflict checking is sufficient; for large-scale deployment, integer-programming formulations are available.

Keystone-refinement tests

The machinery of Sections 3–6 addresses one failure mode of a long-timeline restriction: the restriction is obsolete—the pathway no longer activates when exercised, so the restriction can be lifted entirely. In practice a second failure mode is more common: the restriction is misconfigured—the pathway still activates and lifting the restriction wholesale would remain dangerous, but technological progress has made narrower keystones available that would block the risk pathway without blocking adjacent benign pathways. This section extends the controlled relaxation construction to handle the misconfiguration case.

Keystone structures and their partial order

A restriction \Xi is realised by restricting specific keystone capabilities in the tuple that would otherwise enable the risk pathway . Different choices of which capabilities to restrict may block the same pathway. We make this explicit.

Definition 8 (Keystone structure). Given a risk claim R = (a_j, (d_1, \ldots, d_k), \mathrm{Pathway}, p_R), a keystone structure for R is a subset K\subseteq \{d_1, \ldots, d_k\} whose restriction blocks the exercise pathway \mathrm{Pathway}: if every d \in K is restricted, \mathrm{Pathway} is not exercisable. Write K\vdash \mathrm{Pathway} when this holds.

Definition 9 (Keystone partial order). Given two keystone structures K_1, K_2 that both block \mathrm{Pathway}, write K_1 \preceq K_2 iff K_1 \subseteq K_2. The relation \preceq is a partial order on blocking keystone structures; a \preceq-minimal element is a minimal blocking keystone structure. Restriction cost is monotone along \preceq: narrower structures impose less restriction on adjacent capabilities.

Remark 16 (Technology drift). The set of candidate keystone structures evolves with technology. A keystone that was \preceq-minimal at time t_0 may become non-minimal at t_1 > t_0 if new capability differentiation (for instance, new manufacturing tolerances, new detection methods, or new isolation protocols) produces a finer keystone K' \prec K that still blocks the pathway. The framework’s channel for surfacing such finer structures is the structural discovery value : if structural discovery reveals a new capability that enables a narrower blocking structure, the discovery is value-positive regardless of whether the refinement is subsequently accepted.

Refinement tests as controlled relaxation

A keystone-refinement test probes whether a candidate narrower structure K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \prec K_{\mathrm{current}} still blocks \mathrm{Pathway}.

Definition 10 (Keystone-refinement test). A keystone-refinement test for risk claim R with current restriction realised by K_{\mathrm{current}} is a controlled relaxation test in the slots of Definition 3 with the third slot taken to be the restriction being lifted: T = (R, \Xi_{\mathrm{lifted}}, S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon), where \Xi_{\mathrm{lifted}} is the restriction induced by the keystone difference K_{\mathrm{current}} \setminus K_{\mathrm{narrow}} for some candidate K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \prec K_{\mathrm{current}}. The narrower structure K_{\mathrm{narrow}} remains in force throughout the test as ambient restriction during S; the test asks whether K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \vdash \mathrm{Pathway} holds empirically by exercising only the released keystones. The scope-construction and damage-bound conditions of Definition 3 apply unchanged with \Xi:= \Xi_{\mathrm{lifted}}.

The restriction is not fully lifted; only the keystones outside the narrower structure are released. The risk pathway must therefore route through K_{\mathrm{narrow}} to activate, and K_{\mathrm{narrow}} remains blocking. Under the scope and damage apparatus already proved for CR, the refinement test inherits the damage bound and convergence theorems.

Corollary 5 (Keystone-refinement under CR damage bound). A keystone-refinement test satisfies the damage bound of Theorem 1: worst-case {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction during the test is \leq \epsilon and post-reinstatement is \leq 2 \epsilon. The test is a specialisation of Definition 3 with \Xi:= \Xi_{\mathrm{lifted}} (Definition 10); \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) is computed over pathways that the released keystones permit, holding the unreleased K_{\mathrm{narrow}} restricted as ambient. The scope-bound construction (Definition 4) and the cooperative-closure argument (Theorem 1(c)) apply without modification.

Corollary 6 (Convergence of refinement tests). Under the operating assumptions of Theorem 2 (full-exercise regime), repeated keystone-refinement tests of K_{\mathrm{narrow}} converge almost surely to the correct binary hypothesis: H_0 that K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \vdash \mathrm{Pathway} (the narrower structure blocks the pathway) versus H_1 that it does not. Convergence rate is the KL-divergence rate of Theorem 2(d) applied to the refinement-test observation model.

Remark 17 (Two failure modes of a restriction). A restriction can be wrong in two ways:

  • The pathway no longer activates at all; the risk has disappeared from the current context. Detected by controlled relaxation of the full restriction (Theorem 2): p_R converges to the H_0-consistent value with L_0 < 1 per test.

  • The pathway still activates if the restriction is lifted wholesale, but technological differentiation has made a narrower blocking structure K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \prec K_{\mathrm{current}} available. Detected by keystone-refinement testing (Corollary 6): the current framework finds that K_{\mathrm{narrow}} suffices and the difference keystones can be released.

Both modes are addressed by the same CR apparatus; the distinction is in which restriction the test probes (the full \Xi_{\mathrm{current}} for Mode A, the reduced \Xi_{\mathrm{narrow}} for Mode B). A Mode A failure does not preclude Mode B success against a narrower candidate: a restriction that cannot be fully lifted may still admit refinement.

Remark 18 (Meta-policy for refinement). The test-initiation criteria of Section 7 extend to refinement. The information value of a refinement test is the expected reduction in restriction cost from adopting K_{\mathrm{narrow}} if the test confirms it, minus the test’s expected damage. When a structural argument is available that K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \vdash \mathrm{Pathway} (for instance, a mechanical proof that the released keystones cannot participate in \mathrm{Pathway}), the structural argument is strictly preferable : refinement testing should be reserved for cases where the structural argument is unavailable or contested.

Worked example: nuclear dual-use keystone refinement

The nuclear scenario from  illustrates Mode B in practice. The capability tuple is (d_1, d_2, d_3) = (\text{nuclear physics}, \text{enrichment}, \text{weapons machining}). The exercise pathway \mathrm{Pathway}= “build and detonate a fission weapon” leads to large {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-contraction proportional to the number of agents destroyed.

Historical keystone choice.

Mid-20th-century technology did not operationally distinguish reactor-grade enrichment (3–5% fissile content, using standard centrifuge cascades) from weapons-grade enrichment (90%+, using the same cascade technology reconfigured for higher stage count). A centrifuge cascade capable of the former could in principle be reconfigured for the latter; separation at the level of individual keystones was not feasible. The broad keystone structure K_{\mathrm{current}} = \{d_1, d_2, d_3\} (restrict all three capabilities broadly) was adopted and blocks \mathrm{Pathway} at maximum restriction cost. Under the technology of the time, K_{\mathrm{current}} was \preceq-minimal.

Modern keystone availability.

Subsequent technological differentiation has exposed narrower keystones. Modern isotope-separation technology distinguishes cascade configurations by separative work units (SWU) and stage count in ways that make weapons-grade enrichment identifiable independently of reactor-grade enrichment. Weapons machining (specific tolerance classes for implosion lens fabrication) is likewise separable from general machining. Modern candidate structures include K_{\mathrm{narrow}} = \{d_2^{\mathrm{weapons\text{-}grade}}, d_3^{\mathrm{weapons\text{-}specific}}\} which restricts weapons-grade enrichment cascades and weapons-specific machining tolerances while permitting reactor-grade enrichment and general machining. Under modern technology, K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \prec K_{\mathrm{current}} if K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \vdash \mathrm{Pathway}.

Refinement test.

A keystone-refinement test probes whether K_{\mathrm{narrow}} suffices. The scope S consists of a monitored civilian reactor development programme, exercising the reactor-pathway (reactor-grade enrichment, general machining) under K_{\mathrm{narrow}}; weapons-grade enrichment and weapons-specific machining remain restricted. The detection channel monitors for indicators of weapons-pathway activation: enrichment to weapons-grade fissile content, diversion of reactor feedstock toward weapons-specific cascades, acquisition of weapons-specific machining tolerances. Test duration is set by the time constant of the detection architecture on these indicators; damage tolerance \epsilon bounds the realised contraction even under a diversion attempt bounded by the scope.

Outcomes.

  • If K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \vdash \mathrm{Pathway} (weapons pathway is not exercisable under K_{\mathrm{narrow}}, modern technology hypothesis): the test observes no weapons-pathway activation over the test window, p_R converges toward the consistent hypothesis, and a refined restriction regime adopting K_{\mathrm{narrow}} becomes operationally justified. Civilian reactor development proceeds without collateral restriction.

  • If K_{\mathrm{narrow}} \not\vdash \mathrm{Pathway} (the weapons pathway remains exercisable through some route the narrower structure fails to block—for example, covert diversion of reactor-grade enrichment toward weapons use via routes modern detection would catch but not always): the test observes weapons-pathway activation within S, the safety cutoff fires, damage is bounded by 2 \epsilon (Theorem 1(b)), and K_{\mathrm{current}} remains in force. The refinement test itself is the evidence that broader restriction remains needed under current technology.

Contrast with Mode A testing.

A Mode A test on the nuclear pathway would ask “can K_{\mathrm{current}} be lifted entirely?” The pathway still operates; the Mode A test would detect weapons-pathway activation and correctly reject lifting the restriction. But the Mode A finding does not address the operationally more interesting question: whether the scope of restriction can be refined. Mode B testing is the mechanism for that refinement. The two tests are complementary; the choice is not between them but between whether to perform one, the other, or (in practice) both under a programmatic review schedule.

Applicability beyond nuclear.

The keystone-refinement pattern applies wherever a long-timeline restriction was adopted under technology that did not distinguish benign from risky keystones. Environmental-contamination controls (persistent chemicals whose modern reformulations operate via different mechanisms), export controls on dual-use technology (where modern differentiation allows narrower controls), biomedical restrictions on gain-of-function research (where specific biocontainment tiers separate research protocols), and other regulatory domains exhibit the same structure. In each, the framework’s contribution is to formalise the refinement test as a bounded-damage empirical probe rather than a moratorium-vs.-wholesale-liberalisation binary.

Integration with Prior Results

This section records how controlled relaxation extends the existing GFM machinery: it preserves self-balancing, resolves inter-agent risk disagreement, and extends the multi-channel detection architecture with a sixth, actively generated channel.

Preservation of self-balancing

Proposition 6 (Controlled Relaxation Preserves Self-Balancing). The self-balancing property of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} (, self-balancing of the discounted value under capability dynamics) is preserved under controlled relaxation:

  • During the test window (\Xi lifted within S), the capability tuple (d_1, \ldots, d_k) is exercisable, which by weak-monotonicity axiom M4 of  means {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} is at least as large as under restriction, minus the possible damage bounded by \epsilon. The net effect on {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} is bounded by [-\epsilon, +\Delta_{\mathrm{unrestrict}}] where \Delta_{\mathrm{unrestrict}} \geq 0 is the {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} gain from lifting the restriction.

  • After the test, the updated risk estimate p_R(t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}) more accurately reflects the true risk, so the restriction criterion  is applied against a more accurate estimate. If the risk was genuinely low, the restriction is lifted and {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} permanently increases; if the risk was genuinely high, the restriction is maintained with higher confidence and {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} is unchanged (the restriction was already in place).

  • Over repeated tests (Theorem 2), the risk estimates converge to truth, and the restriction landscape converges to the optimal restriction set—the set of tuples that are genuinely risky. This is a stronger self-balancing guarantee than the static version: not only does {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} self-balance against perturbations, but the restriction landscape itself converges to the truth-tracking configuration.

Part (c) follows from Theorem 2: under Assumptions 4–8, the posterior converges almost surely to the correct hypothesis; iterating this over all restricted tuples yields convergence of the restriction landscape. Technical details in Appendix 12.8. 

Empirical arbitration across disagreeing agents

Proposition 7 (Risk-Claim Consensus via Empirical Arbitration). When multiple agents disagree on the risk of a capability tuple (a_j assigns p_R^{(j)} \gg p_R^{(k)} for agents j, k), controlled relaxation provides an empirical arbitration mechanism.

  • Under max-aggregation of the risk estimates , agent a_j’s high estimate locks the capability. Under log-odds aggregation , the disagreement produces a moderate aggregate that may or may not trigger restriction.

  • In either case, if the capability is restricted, controlled relaxation generates evidence that updates both agents’ estimates toward the truth (Theorem 2). After sufficient tests, the agents’ posteriors converge, and the disagreement is resolved by data rather than by aggregation-rule choice.

  • The convergence rate depends on each agent’s prior weight (how strongly they hold their belief), but Assumption 7 (prior support) and the KL-positivity of Theorem 2(d) guarantee convergence regardless of prior: even a strongly held false risk claim is eventually overwhelmed by repeated controlled-relaxation evidence.

Remark 19 (Test outcome vs. test occurrence). The independence claim above holds at the level of test outcome given test occurrence: O_n is independent of agent reports conditional on the test having been run. Remark 20 (Trust-weight composition with detection integrity). The trust weight of 1 on the CR channel above presupposes that the detection apparatus is correctly calibrated and the scope correctly constructed. Both are themselves subject to integrity constraints. A robust composition caps the CR-channel trust weight by the integrity guarantee of the underlying detection apparatus: w_{\mathrm{CR}} = \min(1, w_{\mathrm{integrity}}), where w_{\mathrm{integrity}} is obtained from the cryptographic commitment apparatus of  when one applies, or from the institutional integrity assessment otherwise. Under this composition, integrity failures in the detection apparatus propagate to the risk estimate with bounded weight rather than full weight; weight-1 trust on the CR channel is the special case w_{\mathrm{integrity}} \geq 1, which is rarely defensible in practice. The composition makes explicit that the CR channel’s trust inherits from the trust mechanism rather than being independent of it.

What this paper accomplishes

Breaks the Wamura problem.

Theorem 2 shows that controlled relaxation drives risk estimates to truth at exponential rate, converting “permanent overcaution” into “temporary overcaution with a characterized resolution time.” This closes the most important named open problem of .

Substitutes experimental evidence for catastrophe-as-teacher.

The classical solution to long-timeline-risk uncertainty is to wait for the first catastrophe to write the historical markers: the tsunami stones of Fudai, the building-code revisions that followed Galveston 1900, the pharmacovigilance regimes that followed thalidomide. That mechanism is available only if the first catastrophe has already been paid for and the markers still survive in a legible form. Neither condition holds for capabilities that did not exist in previous generations—which is precisely the regime the framework’s {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}-maximising agents generate. Controlled relaxation provides experimental evidence with the evidential content historical markers would have recorded—did the pathway activate? how severe was the contraction? which keystones blocked it?—but acquires the evidence within the damage budget \epsilon rather than the cost-of-life that produced historical markers. This is the practical import of Theorem 1: the cost of learning is bounded and chosen by the framework, not paid by the next generation.

Provides a damage-bounded mechanism.

Theorem 1 bounds the maximum damage from a test by the user-controllable parameter \epsilon. Controlled relaxation is therefore safe to deploy: the cost of learning is bounded even when the risk turns out to be real, and the cooperative-closure condition of Definition 4(ii) prevents cross-boundary cascades through shared cooperative capabilities.

Integrates with the existing machinery.

Propositions 6, 7 and Lemma 2 show that the protocol preserves the self-balancing property of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}, resolves inter-agent risk disagreements, and extends the multi-channel detection architecture. The protocol is additive to the existing framework, not a replacement.

Resolves the Loop 3 phase-boundary degeneracy.

The degenerate phase boundary r_{\mathrm{S}}= 0 for Loop 3 in  arises precisely because the Wamura asymmetry eliminates the exogenous correction rate on the restricted dimension. Controlled relaxation makes r_{\mathrm{S}}> 0 for Loop 3 (via the exercise-regime observations of Section 3), restoring the phase boundary to the non-degenerate form that the design criterion of  requires for self-correction. The two papers are complementary: one characterizes when self-correction works in aggregate; this one supplies the micro-mechanism that makes self-correction work for the restriction-specific feedback loop.

Limitations

B-to-C gap inheritance.

All results are stated in terms of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}. Section 1.2 records that \epsilon bounds contraction of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}, not of the experiential target directly; if the gap between them is large, even a controlled-relaxation-clean restriction landscape need not correspond to experiential optimality. The gap-closure programme is a separate research direction, noted in  and outside the scope of this paper.

Honest-channel assumption.

We assume honest observation channels throughout. Adversarial manipulation of the detection signal—a compromised channel reporting fabricated contraction or concealing genuine contraction—falls outside the scope of the proof and is the domain of the commitment protocols of . Controlled relaxation composes cleanly with those protocols—the ledger of  can record test outcomes as committed observations—but the composition itself is not formalized here.

Identifiability under misspecification.

Assumption 5 requires the Bayesian observation model to match the true data-generating process. If \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}} or \beta are misspecified, Theorem 2’s log-likelihood-ratio argument still converges, but it converges to the wrong hypothesis: under the misspecified model, the posterior concentrates on whichever hypothesis is closer in KL-divergence to the true distribution, not on the true hypothesis. A concrete sign-preservation bound delineates the safe region: the convergent hypothesis is correct iff the modeled likelihoods L_0 and L_1 have the correct sign relative to the true distribution—formally, E_{H_0^{\mathrm{true}}}[\log L] < 0 and E_{H_1^{\mathrm{true}}}[\log L] > 0. In the binary model, this reduces to: if Assumption 4 holds for the modeled (\beta, \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) and the true (\beta^\star, \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}^\star) jointly satisfy (1 - \beta^\star)/\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}^\star > 1 and \beta^\star/(1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}^\star) < 1 (i.e., the true detector also has nontrivial power), then the convergent hypothesis is correct, with rate degraded by the KL gap between modeled and true distributions. Misspecification flips the convergent hypothesis only when the modeled detector is qualitatively miscalibrated (modeled L_1 on the wrong side of 1); quantitative misestimation only slows convergence. Detector calibration is therefore a prerequisite for the convergence guarantee to carry its safety interpretation, but the bound is robust to plausible-magnitude calibration error.

Scope-existence for densely cooperative capabilities.

Remark 6 names scope existence as a scheduling precondition that must be enforced by the meta-policy before a CR test is initiated. For application domains where cooperative-capability structure is dense, this precondition is more than a technical caveat. AI capability spaces in particular have the property that almost every capability cooperates with almost every other through the shared computational substrate; in such spaces, \mathrm{max\_contraction}(P) on the full poset may dominate any acceptable \epsilon, and any scope satisfying Definition 4 may be similarly infeasible. We do not have a quantitative estimate for how often feasible scopes exist in AI capability domains. For some risk classes—capabilities with strongly localised cooperative structure (e.g., specific tool-use patterns confined to a domain)—feasible scopes plausibly exist; for others (capabilities with broad cooperative reach, e.g., general reasoning) they may not. When feasible scopes do not exist, the alternative mechanisms named in Remark 6—structural discovery or scope-refinement that trades coverage for feasibility—must be deployed instead. Controlled relaxation is not a universal remedy for the Wamura problem; it is a remedy for the subset of risks where bounded scopes exist.

Open questions

Damage-tolerance calibration.

The tolerance \epsilon is a free parameter that trades off learning speed against risk exposure. Options include

  • a fixed fraction of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} (e.g., \epsilon= 0.01 \cdot {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}});

  • proportional to restriction cost (\epsilon= c \cdot |\Delta {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(\Xi)|);

  • proportional to information value (\epsilon= c' \cdot \mathrm{IV}(T));

  • user-set per test (maximum safety, maximum user burden).

The worked example (Section 8) shows that even small \epsilon produces rapid convergence when detection sensitivity is high, so the answer may depend more on the detection architecture than on \epsilon itself.

Transient versus persistent pathways.

Section 5 assumes persistent pathway activation (Assumption 1). Some risk pathways may be transient—they produce a brief contraction that the detector could miss at low sampling rate. The current formulation handles transient pathways via \beta(\tau_{\mathrm{test}}) = (1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}})^{\min(k, \tau_{\mathrm{test}})} where k is the pathway duration, but if k = 1 then \beta = 1 - \delta_{\mathrm{agg}} may be non-negligible. Whether the protocol should require longer test windows for suspected transient pathways, or whether the detection architecture should raise its sampling rate during tests, is an engineering choice left open here.

Adversarial risk claimants.

Assumption 6(a) rules out adaptation by the tested agent. What happens when an agent strategically inflates risk claims to lock competitors’ capabilities? The controlled relaxation protocol still works (it generates evidence regardless of the claimant’s intent), but the meta-policy for test initiation may need to be more aggressive when strategic inflation is suspected. Detection of strategic inflation is a variant of the scorpion-detection problem : an agent whose risk claims are systematically disconfirmed by controlled-relaxation tests should see its risk-trust {T^{\mathrm{risk}}}_j decrease, eventually down-weighting its future claims in aggregation. Whether the meta-policy should explicitly model this adversarial case, or whether the trust-dynamics response is sufficient, is an open design question.

Proof Details and Technical Notes

This appendix collects the full derivations behind the body’s proof sketches, with particular attention to the rigorous form of the damage bound (Theorem 1) and the almost-sure convergence result (Theorem 2).

Proof of Proposition 1

We record the full Bayesian calculation that underlies the Wamura asymmetry.

Let R denote the event “risk claim R is valid” (equivalently, H_1 holds for the corresponding binary claim) and \neg R its complement. Let C denote the event “contraction observed.” Under the restricted regime with \Xi active, the physical dynamics guarantee \Pr(C \mid R, \Xi) = \Pr(C \mid \neg R, \Xi) = 0: no exercise, no contraction. The likelihood ratio at any time step t is therefore \mathcal{L}(t) = \frac{\Pr(\text{no contraction} \mid R, \Xi)} {\Pr(\text{no contraction} \mid \neg R, \Xi)} = \frac{1 - 0}{1 - 0} = 1. Bayes’ rule gives p_R(t+1) = \frac{p_R(t) \cdot 1} {p_R(t) \cdot 1 + (1 - p_R(t)) \cdot 1} = p_R(t), proving part (b). For part (a), the counterfactual in which the restriction is lifted for a single step and contraction is observed gives \Pr(C \mid R, \text{exercise}) > \Pr(C \mid \neg R, \text{exercise}) (the pathway can activate under R and cannot under \neg R), so the counterfactual update increases p_R.

Proof of Proposition 2 and Corollary 1

Part (a).

The restriction criterion  restricts d_{j^*} when \mathcal{R}_{j^*}(d_1, \ldots, d_k; a_j, t) > |\Delta {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t \mid \text{restrict } d_{j^*})|. For any fixed capability tuple and keystone d_{j^*}, the right-hand side is a bounded constant (restriction cost does not depend on p_R). The left-hand side is monotone in p_R because concentration risk is the probability-weighted expected contraction. There is therefore a threshold p_{\mathrm{thresh}} > 0 above which the restriction fires.

Part (b).

Direct from Proposition 1(b): under the restricted regime the posterior is unchanged.

Part (c).

Consider the set \mathcal{R}(t) = \{\text{restricted tuples at time } t\}. New discoveries may add tuples to \mathcal{R}(t). No mechanism in the existing framework removes tuples: removal would require p_R to drop below p_{\mathrm{thresh}}, which part (b) rules out. Hence \mathcal{R}(t) \subseteq \mathcal{R}(t+1) for all t.

Corollary 1.

The lower bound follows by counting tuples whose initial neutral-prior assignment from at least one agent exceeded p_{\mathrm{thresh}}; these are immediately restricted (part (a)) and remain restricted (part (b)). If the agent distribution is systematically overcautious, the fraction of such tuples grows with the size of the discovered poset.

Proof of Theorem 1 (full derivation)

We supply the rigorous version of the proof sketch in Section 4. The technical content is (i) justifying the finite computation of \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S), (ii) bounding the irreversible component at the safety cutoff, and (iii) showing that cross-boundary cooperative terms are exhaustively covered by the cooperative-closure condition.

Setup.

Fix a test T = (R, \Xi, S, \tau_{\mathrm{test}}, \epsilon) with scope S satisfying Definition 4. Denote by \Pi(S) = \{\mathrm{Pathway}'_1, \ldots, \mathrm{Pathway}'_M\} the finite set of exercise pathways with prerequisites in S and intermediate transitions in S; this set is finite by the bounded-pathway-length property of  and Lemma 1. Denote by \partial S the cooperative surface: the set of cooperative capabilities involving at least one element of S and at least one element of P \setminus S.

Part (a): instantaneous bound.

At any step t' \in \{t, \ldots, t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}\} during the test, the realized contraction is the sum of contractions along activated pathways in \Pi(S) plus any cooperative-surface contributions induced by those activations. By Definition 4(ii), the cooperative-surface contributions are included in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). Any pathway \mathrm{Pathway}' \in \Pi(S) contributes at most its maximum |\Delta {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(\mathrm{Pathway}')|, which by the axioms M1–M6  is a well-defined, finite, non-negative scalar. The total instantaneous contraction is therefore bounded by \max_{\mathrm{Pathway}' \in \Pi(S)} |\Delta {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(\mathrm{Pathway}')| + \text{(cooperative-surface terms)} = \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). Under the scope-selection constraint \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) \leq \epsilon (Definition 3, Phase 1, condition (ii)), we obtain {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'}) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \epsilon.

Part (b): post-cutoff reversibility.

Suppose the safety cutoff triggers at step t' < t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}. The realized contraction at t' decomposes as \Delta {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'} \mid G_t) = \Delta^{\mathrm{rev}}(t' \mid t) + \Delta^{\mathrm{irrev}}(t' \mid t), where \Delta^{\mathrm{rev}} is the contraction recoverable by reinstating \Xi (capability-availability changes) and \Delta^{\mathrm{irrev}} is the irreversible component (information disclosure, trust degradation, non-reversible poset structural changes). Reinstatement at t' + 1 restores \Delta^{\mathrm{rev}} to zero by construction (the restriction re-blocks availability-based pathways), leaving {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'+1}) = {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - \epsilon- \Delta^{\mathrm{irrev}}(t' \mid t). By Definition 4(iii), \Delta^{\mathrm{irrev}}(t' \mid t) \leq \delta_{\mathrm{irrev}}^{\max} \leq \epsilon, giving {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_{t'+1}) \geq {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}}(G_t) - 2\epsilon.

Part (c): scope isolation.

Decompose P into S\sqcup \partial S\sqcup (P \setminus (S\cup \partial S)). For any d \in P \setminus (S\cup \partial S), d is not in any cooperative capability spanning S and P \setminus S, and no pathway in \Pi(S) has prerequisites or intermediate transitions touching d. The test modifies only capability-availability within S (the only capabilities whose status changes), so the induced subgraph of G_t restricted to d’s pathway support is unchanged. Since a pathway’s exercise probability takes G_t as an argument, and the relevant portion of G_t is invariant, the exercise probability of d’s pathways is unchanged during the test. For c \in \partial S, the forward-reachability closure condition Definition 4(ii) guarantees that the expected contraction from any \mathrm{Pathway}_{\mathrm{ext}} in P \setminus S whose prerequisites include a state change in c is already included in \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S). Hence any cross-boundary cascade is bounded by the \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S)-budget and therefore by \epsilon.

Proof of Lemma 1

Pathway length in \Pi(S) is bounded by L (). The number of distinct pathways is at most |S|^L: at each of the L steps we choose one of at most |S| capabilities. For each pathway, the contraction magnitude is computable from the volume axioms M1–M6  in O(|S|) time (enumerating the affected capability tuple). Under the simplifying assumption that cooperative-surface forward-reachability depth is bounded by the same constant L that bounds pathway length (satisfied whenever cross-boundary cascades cannot chain more than pathway-length-many cooperative hops before terminating), enumerating the cooperative surface \partial S and its forward-reachability chains is O(|P|^{L'}). Taking the maximum across enumerated pathways and adding cooperative-surface terms yields \mathrm{max\_contraction}(S) in O(|S|^L \cdot |S| + |P|^{L'}) = O(|S|^{L+1} + |P|^{L'}) time, matching Lemma 1’s statement. When L' \leq L, the second term is dominated and the bound simplifies to O(|P|^{L+1}).

Proof of Theorem 2 (full derivation)

We supply the rigorous a.s.-convergence argument. The sketch in Section 6 reduces to the strong law of large numbers; here we verify the conditions and spell out the quantitative rate.

The log-likelihood random walk.

Define \ell_n = \log \frac{\Pr(O_n \mid H_1)}{\Pr(O_n \mid H_0)} \in \{\log L_1, \log L_0\}, so that \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) + \sum_{i=1}^n \ell_i. Under Assumption 6, the O_n are conditionally i.i.d. given the true hypothesis. Under Assumption 8, every test exercises the capability, so the fixed-binary likelihoods of Section 3 (not the p_{\mathrm{ex}}-discounted partial-exercise forms of Assumption 8) are the correct ones. Under Assumption 5, those likelihoods match the true data-generating distribution. Under Assumption 4, \log L_1\in (0, +\infty) and \log L_0\in (-\infty, 0); both are finite (the likelihoods are bounded strictly away from 0 and 1 because \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\in (0, 1) and \beta \in (0, 1) imply \Pr(O = 0 \mid H_1), \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_0) > 0 and strictly less than 1; the non-degenerate-detector regime of Assumption 4 rules out the boundary cases \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}= 0 and \beta = 0). Hence \ell_n is a bounded i.i.d. sequence.

Drift under each hypothesis.

Under the true hypothesis H_j, \mathbb{E}_{H_j}[\ell_n] = \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_j) \log L_1 + \Pr(O = 0 \mid H_j) \log L_0. Under H_1 this equals \mathrm{KL}(\Pr(\cdot \mid H_1) \,\|\, \Pr(\cdot \mid H_0)) > 0; under H_0 it equals -\mathrm{KL}(\Pr(\cdot \mid H_0) \,\|\, \Pr(\cdot \mid H_1)) < 0. Both are strictly nonzero by Assumption 4 (the two hypothesis-conditional distributions differ, so their KL divergence is strictly positive).

Strong law of large numbers.

By the strong law (e.g., ) applied to the bounded i.i.d. sequence \{\ell_n\}, \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \ell_i \xrightarrow{\mathrm{a.s.}} \mathbb{E}_{H_j}[\ell_n] under the true hypothesis H_j. Hence \sum_{i=1}^n \ell_i \to +\infty a.s. under H_1 and \to -\infty a.s. under H_0. Translating to p_R via the logistic map (p_R= \sigma(\mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R)), which is well-defined at the initial point because p_R(0) \in (0, 1) by Assumption 7 (so \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) is finite), p_R(t_n) \to 1 a.s. under H_1 and \to 0 a.s. under H_0. This establishes Part (a).

Part (b) (run under H_0).

Conditional on all O_i = 0, each \ell_i = \log L_0, so \sum_{i=1}^n \ell_i = n \log L_0. Hence \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) + n \log L_0, and p_R(t_n) \to 0 at geometric rate |\log L_0|. The unconditional statement under H_0 simply replaces “conditional on all O_i = 0” with an expected drift (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) \log L_0+ \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\log L_1, which is -\mathrm{KL}(\Pr(\cdot \mid H_0) \,\|\, \Pr(\cdot \mid H_1)); again strictly negative.

Part (c) (rate under H_1).

Each test produces O_n = 1 with probability q_1 = \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_1) = \theta_1(1 - \beta) + (1 - \theta_1)\alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}. The first detection is a geometrically distributed stopping time \tau_1 with mean 1/q_1. The log-odds update on O = 1 is \log L_1> 0. For \theta_1 close to 1 and small \beta, a single detection update \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R+ \log L_1 exceeds any fixed threshold \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}(1 - \delta), so the expected time to reach p_R> 1 - \delta is O(1/q_1) = O(1/[\theta_1(1 - \beta)]) as stated.

Part (d) (KL form).

The drift-rate statements above rewrite to \begin{align*} H_0: \ -\mathbb{E}_{H_0}[\ell_n] &= (1 - \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}) \log(1/L_0) + \alpha_{\mathrm{fp}}\log(1/L_1) \\ &= \mathrm{KL}(\Pr(\cdot \mid H_0) \,\|\, \Pr(\cdot \mid H_1)), \\ H_1: \ \mathbb{E}_{H_1}[\ell_n] &= \Pr(O = 1 \mid H_1) \log L_1 + \Pr(O = 0 \mid H_1) \log L_0\\ &= \mathrm{KL}(\Pr(\cdot \mid H_1) \,\|\, \Pr(\cdot \mid H_0)). \end{align*} Both are strictly positive under Assumption 4, so Chernoff’s large-deviations result  gives the corresponding exponent on the probability of misclassification: \Pr(p_R(t_n) < 1/2 \mid H_1) decays at least as fast as \exp(-n \cdot \mathrm{KL}_{H_1}) for some \mathrm{KL}_{H_1} > 0, and symmetrically for H_0.

Proof of Corollary 3

Under H_0 with all n tests producing O_n = 0, \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) = \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0) + n \log L_0. Setting \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(t_n) \leq \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_{\mathrm{clear}} and solving for n: n \geq \frac{\mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_{\mathrm{clear}} - \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}p_R(0)} {\log L_0}. Using \mathop{\mathrm{logit}}x = \log(x/(1-x)), the numerator equals \log(p_{\mathrm{clear}}(1 - p_R(0)) / (p_R(0)(1 - p_{\mathrm{clear}}))). Note \log L_0< 0, so the inequality direction flips when dividing; taking the ceiling gives .

Proof of Proposition 6

Part (a).

Axiom M4 (weak monotonicity) of {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} gives that adding capabilities to P (equivalently, lifting a restriction) never decreases {\mathop{\mathrm{vol}}_{\mathrm{P}}} ignoring risk effects. Subtracting the worst-case risk effect, bounded by \epsilon during the test and by 2\epsilon post-cutoff (Theorem 1), gives the stated interval.

Part (b).

The restriction criterion  depends on p_R; replacing the pre-test p_R with the post-test p_R(t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}) produces a criterion evaluated against a more accurate estimate. If p_R(t + \tau_{\mathrm{test}}) < p_{\mathrm{thresh}}, the criterion no longer fires and the restriction is lifted; otherwise it is maintained.

Part (c).

Theorem 2 establishes that p_R(t_n) \to \mathbf{1}_{H_1} a.s. for each tuple. Since the restriction criterion is continuous in p_R and restricts iff p_R\geq p_{\mathrm{thresh}}, the restriction decisions converge to \{\text{restrict} : \text{genuinely risky}\} a.s.
