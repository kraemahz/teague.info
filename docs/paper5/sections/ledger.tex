\section{Ledger Architecture}
\label{sec:ledger}

The commitment protocol of \S\ref{sec:commitment} requires a persistent,
tamper-evident record of commitments, verification results, and
governance decisions.  This section specifies the minimal ledger
architecture that satisfies those requirements.

\subsection{Design Principles}
\label{sec:ledger_principles}

The ledger is motivated by verification requirements, not by analogy to
financial blockchains.  Its design is driven by three properties that
the commitment protocol needs and constrained by what the protocol does
\emph{not} need.

\paragraph{Required properties.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Append-only:} Entries cannot be deleted or modified
    after recording.  This ensures that commitments, once published,
    remain binding---an agent cannot retroactively alter a commitment
    to match a different claim.
    \item \textbf{Publicly auditable:} Any ledger participant can verify
    the integrity of the entire chain from genesis to the present.
    This enables third-party audit of both individual verification
    records and aggregate patterns (e.g., an agent whose claims are
    systematically falsified across benchmarks).
    \item \textbf{Substrate-distributed:} The ledger is replicated
    across nodes on different substrates.  No single substrate controls
    the canonical copy.  This prevents a same-substrate coalition from
    modifying or censoring verification records.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Not required.}
The verification ledger does \emph{not} require token economics,
proof-of-work or proof-of-stake consensus, smart contract execution, or
high transaction throughput.  Governance decisions are infrequent
(monthly or less); per-claim verification entries are more frequent but
do not require global consensus (see \S\ref{sec:ledger_consensus}).
The minimal construction is an authenticated append-only data
structure---a Merkle hash tree~\citep{crosby2009efficient}---replicated
across substrate-exclusive nodes.

\subsection{Verification Entries}
\label{sec:ver_entries}

\begin{definition}[Verification Ledger Entry]
\label{def:ver_entry}
A \textbf{verification entry} in $\Ledger$ is a tuple
\[
    e_{\mathrm{ver}} = (a_j,\; \lambda,\; w,\; B,\; v,\; \xi,\;
    t,\; \sigma_a,\; \sigma_w)
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a_j$ is the agent identifier,
    \item $\lambda = \Commit(C, r)$ is the commitment,
    \item $w$ identifies the algorithmic witness
    $\Witness_{\mathrm{alg}}$,
    \item $B$ is the governance-accepted benchmark identifier,
    \item $v \in \{\texttt{pass}, \texttt{fail}\}$ is the verdict,
    \item $\xi$ is a hash of the supporting evidence (raw data is
    stored off-chain; the hash ensures integrity),
    \item $t$ is the timestamp,
    \item $\sigma_a$ is the agent's signature over $\lambda$ (from the
    Commit phase), and
    \item $\sigma_w$ is the witness's signature over $(v, \xi, t)$
    (from the Verify phase).
\end{itemize}
The entry is completed when $a_j$ opens the commitment by publishing
$(C, r)$, at which point any participant can verify $\Commit(C, r) =
\lambda$.
\end{definition}

Verification entries require agreement between exactly two parties: the
committing agent (who signs the commitment) and the verifying witness
(who signs the verdict).  This two-party, cross-substrate agreement is
sufficient for per-claim records and is simpler than general
multi-party consensus.

\subsection{Governance Entries}
\label{sec:gov_entries}

\begin{definition}[Governance Ledger Entry]
\label{def:gov_entry}
A \textbf{governance entry} in $\Ledger$ is a tuple
\[
    e_{\mathrm{gov}} = (B,\; \sigma_B,\; \tau,\; p,\;
    \mathcal{D},\; \mathcal{V},\; \mathrm{outcome},\; t_{\mathrm{eff}})
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $B$ is the benchmark identifier,
    \item $\sigma_B$ is the benchmark specification (what it tests,
    scoring criteria, passing threshold),
    \item $\tau$ is the target capability type,
    \item $p$ is the proposer identifier,
    \item $\mathcal{D}$ is the deliberation record (all analysis
    submitted during the deliberation window, with submitter
    identifiers and timestamps),
    \item $\mathcal{V}$ is the vote record (per-validator votes with
    substrate identifiers),
    \item $\mathrm{outcome} \in \{\texttt{accepted},
    \texttt{rejected}\}$, and
    \item $t_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the effective date (for accepted
    benchmarks; null for rejected ones).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The governance record is permanent: rejected proposals remain in the
ledger with their full deliberation records.  This provides an audit
trail for benchmark evolution and prevents re-proposal of
previously-studied-and-rejected benchmarks without presenting new
evidence.  The deliberation record is itself a public good: future
benchmark designers can study past proposals' failure modes.

\subsection{Consensus Among Substrate-Exclusive Nodes}
\label{sec:ledger_consensus}

The ledger's consensus requirements differ between entry types,
reflecting their different trust models and frequency.

\paragraph{Verification entries.}
Appending a verification entry requires agreement between the committing
agent $a_j$ and the verifying witness $w$---a two-party cross-substrate
protocol.  No global consensus is needed.  The entry is self-verifying:
any participant can check the signatures and, after the commitment is
opened, verify that $\Commit(C, r) = \lambda$.  Conflicts (e.g., an
agent claiming the witness misreported the verdict) are resolvable by
replaying the exercise protocol against the committed data, which is
deterministic by Definition~\ref{def:alg_witness}.

\paragraph{Governance entries.}
Appending a governance entry (benchmark acceptance) requires a
cross-substrate supermajority as specified in
Definition~\ref{def:gov_fork}: at least fraction $\theta$ of validators
overall, with at least one validator per represented substrate.  This is
a special-purpose consensus that is simpler than general Byzantine fault
tolerance because: (a)~governance entries are infrequent (monthly or
less), so latency tolerance is high; (b)~the vote is over a
well-defined proposal with a fixed deliberation record, not an
arbitrary state transition; and (c)~the cross-substrate requirement is a
structural constraint (ensured by the substrate partition) rather than a
probabilistic one (ensured by random committee selection).

\subsection{Relation to the GFM Harness Ledger}
\label{sec:harness_relation}

The GFM harness maintains a capability ledger that tracks agents,
individual capabilities, trust states, and cooperative capabilities.
Paper~5's construction provides the cryptographic infrastructure that
makes this ledger tamper-evident, cross-substrate verifiable, and
governance-auditable.  The relationship is layered: the harness ledger
is the \emph{application layer} (what is tracked), and the
verification ledger of this section is the \emph{integrity layer}
(how tracking is made trustworthy).  A harness ledger entry claiming
``agent $a_j$ has capability $c_k$ with trust $T_j$'' is grounded when
the verification ledger contains a corresponding entry showing that
$c_k$ was committed, exercised by a substrate-exclusive witness, and
confirmed.
