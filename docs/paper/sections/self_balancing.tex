\section{The Self-Balancing Property}
\label{sec:self_balancing}

The central claim of this paper is that GFM's single objective---maximizing $\vol(G)$---simultaneously penalizes destruction, coercion, and rigidity, all from the same geometric argument. The argument is measure monotonicity: removing elements from a measurable set cannot increase its measure. We develop this claim through three lemmas establishing the anti-maximizing character of each failure mode, then synthesize them in Proposition~\ref{prop:self_balancing} as a structural observation about the shape of the objective.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Lemma 1: Destructive actions
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{lemma}[Net Effect of Elimination]
\label{lem:destruction}
Let $a_k$ be an agent in a population $\{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ with joint goal space $G$ as defined in Equation~\eqref{eq:joint_goal_space}. If an action $\pi$ eliminates $a_k$, the change in volume is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:destruction_bound}
\Delta \vol(G) \;=\; \vol\!\bigl(G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}\bigr) \;-\; \vol\!\Bigl(\Gind{k} \setminus \bigcup_{j \neq k} \Gind{j}\Bigr) \;-\; \vol\!\bigl(G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k}\bigr)
\end{equation}
where $G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k} \subseteq \Gcoop$ denotes cooperative capabilities that require $a_k$'s participation and $G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}$ denotes cooperative capabilities among the remaining agents that $a_k$'s presence was suppressing.
\end{lemma}

\emph{Condition.} Elimination is net-contracting when $a_k$'s positive contributions---unique individual capabilities and cooperative capabilities it enables---exceed the cooperative capabilities its presence suppresses: $\vol(\Gind{k} \setminus \bigcup_{j \neq k} \Gind{j}) + \vol(G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k}) > \vol(G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k})$. For most agents this holds comfortably, since the suppression term $G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}$ is typically zero or small. But for a scorpion actively disrupting cooperation between other agents, $G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}$ can be large---removing the disruptive agent unlocks cooperative capabilities that its interference was preventing. This is precisely the mechanism that makes scorpion containment $\vol(G)$-serving (Section~\ref{subsec:scorpion_interaction}).

\emph{Proof sketch.} The joint goal space before removal is $G = \bigl(\bigcup_k \Gind{k}\bigr) \cup \Gcoop$. After removing $a_k$, the new joint space is $G' = \bigl(\bigcup_{j \neq k} \Gind{j}\bigr) \cup \bigl(\Gcoop \setminus G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k}\bigr) \cup G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}$, where the last term represents cooperative capabilities newly realizable among the remaining agents. The volume change is $\vol(G') - \vol(G)$: the gain from $G^{\mathrm{coop}+}_{k}$ minus the loss from $a_k$'s unique individual contributions and the cooperative capabilities that required its participation. \qed

The bound accounts for both the immediate geometric loss and the suppression release. It does not capture second-order cascading effects: the remaining agents may lose access to cooperative capabilities that depended on $a_k$ as an intermediary, or may gain additional cooperative capabilities as the population structure adjusts. These dynamics are real but depend on the specific topology of agent interactions, which the lemma does not model.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Lemma 2: Coercive actions
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{lemma}[Coercive Actions are Anti-Maximizing, with Caveat]
\label{lem:coercion}
If an action $\pi$ restricts agent $a_j$'s achievable capabilities, replacing $\Gind{j}$ with ${\Gind{j}}' \subset \Gind{j}$, then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coercion_bound}
\Delta \vol(G) \;\leq\; \vol(G') - \vol(G) \;\leq\; 0
\end{equation}
where $G' = \bigl(\bigcup_{i \neq j} \Gind{i} \cup {\Gind{j}}'\bigr) \cup {\Gcoop}'$ and ${\Gcoop}' \subseteq \Gcoop$ excludes any cooperative capabilities rendered unrealizable by the restriction. The inequality is strict whenever $a_j$'s restriction removes capabilities not covered by other agents.
\end{lemma}

\emph{Proof sketch.} Since ${\Gind{j}}' \subset \Gind{j}$ and ${\Gcoop}' \subseteq \Gcoop$, the restricted joint space satisfies $G' \subseteq G$. Measure monotonicity gives the result. \qed

\emph{The caveat.} Lemma~\ref{lem:coercion} states a \emph{ceteris paribus} result: restricting $a_j$ in isolation contracts $\vol(G)$. But actions rarely occur in isolation. Restricting $a_j$ to protect $a_k$ may produce a net expansion if $\Delta \vol(\Gind{k}) > |\Delta \vol(\Gind{j})|$---that is, if the capabilities preserved or created for $a_k$ exceed the capabilities removed from $a_j$. A quarantine that temporarily restricts movement to prevent a pandemic may be net-expanding: the capabilities preserved by keeping agents alive and healthy outweigh the temporary restriction on movement. An authoritarian state that permanently restricts many agents' capabilities to protect a ruling class is net-contracting: the concentration of capabilities in a few agents cannot compensate for the broad contraction across the population, because independent capability sets are additive under Definition~\ref{def:social_objective}.

The claim is not that coercion is never justified under GFM. The claim is that GFM makes the tradeoff \emph{explicit and measurable}. An action that restricts $a_j$ must demonstrate a compensating expansion in $\vol(G)$---not in the restricting agent's own capability set, but in the joint space. Where deontological rules either prohibit all coercion (and fail under adversarial conditions) or permit it by fiat (and provide no bound on its scope), GFM requires a geometric accounting: the restriction must be net-expanding over $G$ as a whole.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Lemma 3: Self-imposed rigidity
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{lemma}[Self-Imposed Rigidity is Anti-Maximizing]
\label{lem:rigidity}
Let $A$ denote a GFM actor's action space and let $R \subset A$ be a set of rigid rules that restricts the actor to the subspace $A \setminus R$. If the actor's unrestricted action space contains actions that would expand $\vol(G)$, then the restricted actor achieves
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rigidity_bound}
\max_{\pi \in A \setminus R} \mathbb{E}[\Delta \vol(G) \mid \pi] \;\leq\; \max_{\pi \in A} \mathbb{E}[\Delta \vol(G) \mid \pi]
\end{equation}
with strict inequality whenever $R$ excludes at least one $\vol(G)$-expanding action not available in $A \setminus R$.
\end{lemma}

\emph{Condition.} The rigidity cost is non-trivial when the actor's action space contributes to $\vol(G)$. An actor with no influence on the joint goal space---no capability to expand or contract any agent's capabilities---has zero rigidity cost. The cost grows with the actor's influence: a powerful actor operating under rigid constraints forgoes more $\vol(G)$-expanding opportunities than a weak one.

\emph{Proof sketch.} Optimizing over a subset of a set cannot exceed optimizing over the full set. The restricted maximum in Equation~\eqref{eq:rigidity_bound} is bounded above by the unrestricted maximum. When $R$ excludes actions that would be optimal under unrestricted optimization, the inequality is strict. \qed

Rigidity costs are not merely theoretical. An actor bound by fixed rules cannot weigh the probabilities of uncertain scenarios, cannot adapt to adversarial conditions that exploit the rules' predictability, and cannot take calculated risks when the expected $\vol(G)$ expansion justifies the risk. We develop the sharpest illustration of this cost in Appendix~\ref{app:evil_twin}, where a rule-bound agent faces an adversary who exploits the rules' rigidity to force a $\vol(G)$-contracting outcome.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Proposition 1: Self-Balancing Property (synthesizes the lemmas)
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{proposition}[Self-Balancing Property]
\label{prop:self_balancing}
For a GFM actor optimizing $\vol(G)$ over a population of agents, under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:social_objective}, the following structural property holds: the same objective function penalizes both extremes of the permissiveness--control spectrum.

\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{(a)}] \textbf{Destructive permissiveness is anti-maximizing.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:destruction}, actions that eliminate agents or destroy capabilities contract $\vol(G)$. A GFM actor will not pursue destruction unless a simultaneous expansion elsewhere exceeds the contraction---and must demonstrate this geometrically, not assert it.

\item[\textbf{(b)}] \textbf{Coercive restriction is anti-maximizing.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:coercion}, actions that restrict other agents' capabilities contract $\vol(G)$ unless compensated by a larger expansion. Broad, sustained restriction---the authoritarian pattern---is net-contracting under the additivity assumption of Definition~\ref{def:social_objective}. Narrow, temporary restriction---the quarantine pattern---may be net-expanding when it preserves more capabilities than it restricts.

\item[\textbf{(c)}] \textbf{Self-imposed rigidity is anti-maximizing.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}, constraining the actor's own action space reduces its ability to maximize $\vol(G)$. Rigid rules create exploitable predictability (Appendix~\ref{app:evil_twin}) and prevent adaptation under uncertainty.

\item[\textbf{(d)}] \textbf{Structural balance.} Parts (a)--(c) together establish that the $\vol(G)$ objective creates pressure away from \emph{both} destructive permissiveness (which contracts $G$ directly) \emph{and} overrestrictive control (which contracts $G$ through coercion and rigidity). A GFM actor that destroys too freely violates (a); one that restricts too broadly violates (b); one that follows rules too rigidly violates (c). The single objective captures all three failure modes without requiring separate mechanisms for safety and freedom.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

Part (d) is a structural claim about the \emph{shape} of the objective, not an equilibrium existence proof. Proving that the opposing pressures of (a)--(c) produce stable interior equilibria would require additional assumptions: continuity of $\vol(G)$ with respect to actions, compactness of the action space, and specific structure on the tradeoffs between expansion and contraction. These assumptions may hold in particular instantiations of GFM but are not provided by the framework in its current generality. What the proposition establishes is weaker but still valuable: the objective penalizes both extremes from the same geometric principle. Whether the resulting pressure produces a stable balance point, oscillation around one, or convergent dynamics depends on the specifics of a given system---and characterizing those dynamics is an open problem.

% Corollary 1.1: Elimination is almost always anti-maximizing
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{corollary}[Elimination is Almost Always Anti-Maximizing]
\label{cor:elimination}
For any agent $a_k$ with at least one capability $g \in \Gind{k}$ such that $g \notin \Gind{j}$ for some $j \neq k$, the unique individual contribution $\vol(\Gind{k} \setminus \bigcup_{j \neq k} \Gind{j})$ is strictly positive. Furthermore, the cooperative loss $\vol(G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k})$ grows with the number of agents that interact with $a_k$: each interaction partner contributes at least one cooperative capability to $G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k}$, and the number of distinct multi-agent coalitions involving $a_k$ grows combinatorially with the number of partners.
\end{corollary}

\emph{Proof sketch.} The first claim follows from the definition of a measurable set: a non-empty set with a well-defined measure has strictly positive volume (assuming $\G$ has no atoms of zero measure at the capability granularity, which is a standing assumption of the framework). For the cooperative claim, consider the coalitions involving $a_k$. Each subset $S \subseteq \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ with $a_k \in S$ and $|S| \geq 2$ can generate cooperative capabilities in $G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k}$. The number of such subsets is $2^{n-1} - 1$. Not every subset generates unique cooperative capabilities, but under the reasonable assumption that at least some multi-agent coalitions produce capabilities not achievable by their proper subcoalitions, $\vol(G^{\mathrm{coop}}_{k})$ grows faster than linearly in the number of interaction partners. The precise scaling depends on the structure of cooperation in a given population and is not characterized here. \qed

The combinatorial observation is not a formal hardness result---it is a motivating argument for why the cost of elimination is typically much larger than the cost of the individual capabilities lost. A surgeon who is eliminated loses not only their personal skills but every surgery they could have performed with every anesthesiologist, every operation requiring their specific expertise in combination with specific equipment, and every training relationship with every student. The cooperative web radiates outward from each agent, and severing a node severs all its connections.

% Corollary 1.2: Rigid rules are anti-maximizing

\begin{corollary}[Rigid Rules are Anti-Maximizing]
\label{cor:rigid_rules}
Let $R$ be a fixed rule set imposed on a GFM actor, and let $A$ be the actor's unrestricted action space. If there exist states of the world in which the optimal $\vol(G)$-expanding action $\pi^* \in R$ (i.e., the optimal action is excluded by the rules), then the rule-bound actor is strictly suboptimal with respect to the GFM objective.
\end{corollary}

\emph{Proof sketch.} Direct from Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}: optimizing over $A \setminus R$ when $\pi^* \in R$ yields strict inequality in Equation~\eqref{eq:rigidity_bound}. The question is whether such states exist. For any non-trivial rule set $R$ operating in an adversarial environment, the answer is generically yes: an adversary with knowledge of $R$ can construct scenarios in which the rules' constraints force the actor into a $\vol(G)$-contracting response. We provide a concrete construction in Appendix~\ref{app:evil_twin}. \qed

Corollary~\ref{cor:rigid_rules} does not claim that all rules are harmful. Heuristic rules that approximate $\vol(G)$-maximizing behavior---``do not kill'' as an approximation of Lemma~\ref{lem:destruction}'s geometric result---are useful defaults. The corollary claims that \emph{rigid, inviolable} rules are strictly suboptimal: there exist situations where breaking the rule would better serve the objective. A GFM actor treats such rules as strong priors, not as hard constraints, and can override them when the geometric accounting demonstrates a net expansion in $\vol(G)$.

\begin{proposition}[Scorpion Detection under Observable Contraction]
\label{prop:scorpion_detection}
Let $a_s$ be an agent whose actions persistently contract $\vol(G)$---a \emph{scorpion}, in the sense defined below. A GFM actor maintaining observable goal models $M_k(G)$ (Definition~\ref{def:observable_goal_model}) will detect $a_s$'s $\vol(G)$-contracting behavior to the extent that the contraction is observable through individually measurable capability changes. Formally: if $a_s$'s actions produce a persistent pattern $\Delta \vol(\Gind{k}) < 0$ across agents $a_k$ in the GFM actor's observation set, the local volume estimator (Definition~7, Section~\ref{sec:tractability}) will register a persistent negative $\Deltahat$, and the trust model will decrease $T_s$ for agent $a_s$ over successive observations.
\end{proposition}

A \emph{scorpion}---an agent that defects against the cooperative structure regardless of the rewards available through cooperation---is defined by its persistent $\vol(G)$-contracting behavior. The scorpion taxonomy includes cornered animals (agents forced into defection by circumstance), rational scorpions (agents whose private objectives diverge from cooperative play), and ideological scorpions (agents operating under value systems orthogonal to the population's). All produce the same observable signature: persistent contraction of the joint capability space. We defer the full taxonomy to Appendix~\ref{app:scorpion_taxonomy}.

\emph{Proof sketch.} Under Definition~\ref{def:observable_goal_model}, the GFM actor observes changes in each agent's individual capability set through communication, behavior, and environmental signals. When $a_s$ persistently reduces other agents' capabilities---destroying resources, coercing agents, disrupting cooperation---the affected agents' capability sets contract: ${\Gind{k}}' \subset \Gind{k}$ for agents $a_k$ in $a_s$'s vicinity. These contractions are individually observable, producing negative $\Delta \vol(\Gind{k})$ signals that aggregate into a persistent negative $\Deltahat$ in the local estimator. The trust update mechanism attributes persistent contraction to its source: $T_s$ decreases as the pattern accumulates, eventually flagging $a_s$ as a net-contracting agent. \qed

\begin{remark}[Scope Limitation]
\label{rem:detection_scope}
Proposition~\ref{prop:scorpion_detection} inherits the observability constraints that will be established in Proposition~3 (Section~\ref{sec:tractability}). The detection mechanism is strongest for \emph{direct} scorpions---those whose actions produce visible contraction in individually observable capability sets. It is weakest for \emph{social} and \emph{structural} scorpions that operate primarily through coalition disruption, trust degradation, or observability reduction: agents that contract $\Gcoop$ or degrade the estimator's signal quality without directly reducing any individual $\Gind{k}$. Such agents may evade detection for extended periods, particularly when their contracting actions are distributed across many small interactions below the estimator's noise floor. The proposition makes no claim about convergence rate: a slow-moving scorpion operating near the detection threshold may persist undetected for longer than a fast-moving one. Identifying social and structural scorpions is an open problem, not a solved one.
\end{remark}
